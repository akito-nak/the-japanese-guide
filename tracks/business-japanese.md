# Track: Business Japanese 💼

> *"Japanese business culture is not just 'polite Japanese turned up to 11.' It has its own vocabulary, its own rituals, its own written forms, and its own unspoken rules. Get it right and you open remarkable doors."*

This track is for learners headed to Japanese workplaces, dealing with Japanese clients and partners, preparing for Japanese job interviews, or working in international environments with Japanese colleagues. Business Japanese (ビジネス日本語) is a distinct register that requires deliberate study — even fluent speakers of casual Japanese need to learn it separately.

---

## Why Business Japanese Is Its Own World

Business Japanese differs from everyday polite Japanese in four key ways:

1. **Vocabulary:** Entirely different set of phrases, set expressions, and honorific forms
2. **Written conventions:** Formal email structures, specific opening/closing phrases
3. **Implicit rules:** What you say and what you mean can be very different
4. **Hierarchy encoding:** Every communication signals your position and theirs

You cannot just "be polite" your way through a Japanese business setting. Specific formulas are expected and deviating from them reads as inexperienced or disrespectful.

## Business Card Etiquette — 名刺交換 (Meishi Kōkan)

The business card exchange is a ritual with specific choreography:

1. **Prepare:** Cards in your card holder (名刺入れ), never in your pocket or wallet
2. **Bow and present:** With both hands, card facing them (text toward them), slight bow
3. **Receive:** With both hands, read it carefully, acknowledge it:
   > 頂戴いたします。(Chōdai itashimasu.) — "I receive this humbly."
4. **Place respectfully:** On the table in front of you during a meeting, or in your card holder
5. **Never:** Write on it, fold it, leave it on the table when you leave, put it in your back pocket

At multi-person meetings, arrange received cards on the table in front of you corresponding to seating positions.

## Meeting Phrases

### Starting a Meeting
> 本日はお時間をいただきありがとうございます。
> (Honjitsu wa ojikan wo itadaki arigatō gozaimasu.)
> Thank you for taking the time to meet with us today.

> 早速ですが、本題に入らせていただきます。
> (Sassoku desu ga, hondai ni irasete itadakimasu.)
> Shall we get to the main topic?

> 本日の議題は〜です。
> (Honjitsu no gidai wa ~ desu.)
> Today's agenda is ~.

### Asking for Clarification
> もう少し詳しくお聞かせいただけますか？
> (Mō sukoshi kuwashiku okikase itadakemasu ka?)
> Could you elaborate on that a bit more?

> おっしゃる意味がよく分かりませんでした。
> (Ossharu imi ga yoku wakarimasen deshita.)
> I didn't quite understand what you meant.

### Agreeing and Acknowledging
> おっしゃる通りです。
> (Ossharu tōri desu.)
> Exactly as you say.

> 承知いたしました。
> (Shōchi itashimashita.)
> Understood. I'll take care of it.

> かしこまりました。
> (Kashikomarimashita.)
> Certainly. (Extremely formal)

### Politely Declining or Pushing Back
> ご提案はごもっともですが、弊社の状況といたしましては...
> (Go-teian wa gomottomo desu ga, heisha no jōkyō to itashimashite wa...)
> Your proposal is very reasonable, but given our company's situation...

> 検討させていただきます。
> (Kentō sasete itadakimasu.)
> We will consider it. (Often signals "we'll think of a way to say no")

> 難しい状況です。
> (Muzukashii jōkyō desu.)
> The situation is difficult. (= probably no)

### Closing a Meeting
> 本日は大変参考になりました。
> (Honjitsu wa taihen sankō ni narimashita.)
> Today's meeting was very informative.

> それでは、今後ともよろしくお願いいたします。
> (Soredewa, kongotomo yoroshiku onegai itashimasu.)
> Please continue to treat us well in the future.

### Key Email Phrases

**Openers:**
| Phrase | When to use |
|---|---|
| お世話になっております | Standard business opener — always |
| いつもお世話になっております | Emphasizes ongoing relationship |
| ご無沙汰しております | "It's been a while" — reconnecting |
| 初めてご連絡いたします | First contact |
| 突然のご連絡失礼いたします | Cold outreach apology |

**Body transitions:**
| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 早速ですが | Getting straight to the point |
| つきましては | "In light of this..." / "Therefore..." |
| なお | "Additionally..." |
| また | "Also..." |
| ご確認いただきたく... | "I would like to ask for your confirmation..." |

**Closers:**
| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| よろしくお願いいたします | Best regards / Please take care of this |
| ご確認よろしくお願いいたします | Please kindly check |
| お返事お待ちしております | I look forward to your reply |
| 何かご不明な点がございましたらお気軽に | If you have questions, feel free |
| ご多忙のところ恐縮ですが | I'm sorry to trouble you when you're busy |

## The 報連相 (Hōrenso) System

**報告 (hōkoku) — 連絡 (renraku) — 相談 (sōdan)**

This is the fundamental communication framework of Japanese business:

- **報告 (report):** Update your superior on the progress/outcome of tasks they assigned
- **連絡 (contact):** Share information relevant to others in the team proactively
- **相談 (consult):** Bring problems or decisions to your superior *before* acting unilaterally

The 報連相 system means Japanese workers are expected to over-communicate compared to Western workplaces. Surprises are bad. Proactive communication is good.

Bonus: **確認 (kakunin — confirmation)** is sometimes added: 報連相確 (hōrensō-kaku).

## 根回し (Nemawashi) — The Hidden Meeting Before the Meeting

**根回し** literally means "going around the roots" — as in, preparing the soil before planting. In business, it refers to informal pre-meeting consensus building.

Before a formal decision is made in a meeting, Japanese professionals typically:
1. Have one-on-one conversations with key stakeholders
2. Share documents and ideas informally beforehand
3. Gauge objections and modify proposals accordingly
4. Arrive at the formal meeting with consensus already built

The formal meeting is then mostly a formality — everyone has already agreed. This explains why Japanese meetings can seem "ceremonial" to Westerners — because they are. The real decisions happened before the meeting.

Understanding nemawashi prevents massive cultural frustration: proposals introduced "cold" in a Japanese meeting without prior consultation will often stall or fail.

## Job Interview Japanese

### Self-Introduction (自己紹介)
> 私は〇〇大学〇〇学部を卒業し、〇〇株式会社に〇年間勤めておりました。主に〇〇業務を担当し、〜の経験を積んでまいりました。
> "I graduated from 〇〇 University's School of 〇〇, and worked at 〇〇 Company for ~ years. My main responsibilities were 〇〇, and I have accumulated experience in ~."

### Common Interview Questions
| Question | Japanese |
|---|---|
| "Tell me about yourself" | 自己紹介をお願いします |
| "Why do you want this job?" | 志望動機をお聞かせください |
| "What are your strengths?" | あなたの長所は何ですか？|
| "What are your weaknesses?" | 短所についてお聞かせください |
| "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" | 5年後のビジョンを教えてください |

### Answer Structure (STAR Method in Japanese)
- **状況 (jōkyō):** The situation
- **課題 (kadai):** The challenge/task
- **行動 (kōdō):** The action I took
- **結果 (kekka):** The result

## Japanese Company Culture to Know

**年功序列 (nenkō joretsu):** Seniority system — pay and position based largely on years of service. Fading but still influential.

**終身雇用 (shūshin koyō):** Lifetime employment — one company, one career. Also fading in the modern economy.

**飲みニケーション (nominication):** Communication through drinking (飲む + communication). After-work drinks with colleagues (飲み会 — nomi kai) are semi-mandatory social bonding.

**有給が取れない (yūkyū ga torenai):** Unable to take paid vacation — common complaint. Japan legally provides vacation but cultural pressure to not use it is strong.

**ブラック企業 (burakku kigyō):** "Black company" — exploitative employer. Antonym: ホワイト企業 (howaito kigyō).
