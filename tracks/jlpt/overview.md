# JLPT — The Japanese Language Proficiency Test

> *"The JLPT won't make you fluent. But it gives you a roadmap, a milestone, and a certificate that Japan actually recognizes. That combination is hard to beat."*

The **日本語能力試験 (Nihongo Nōryoku Shiken — Japanese Language Proficiency Test)** is the world's most widely taken Japanese certification exam, administered by the Japan Foundation and Japan Educational Exchanges and Services (JEES). It's held twice a year — July and December — at locations worldwide.

---

## The Five Levels

| Level | Label | Vocabulary | Kanji | Study hours (est.) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| N5 | Beginner | ~800 words | ~100 | 150–200 hours |
| N4 | Elementary | ~1,500 words | ~300 | 300–500 hours |
| N3 | Intermediate | ~3,750 words | ~650 | 600–900 hours |
| N2 | Upper-Intermediate | ~6,000 words | ~1,000 | 1,000–1,500 hours |
| N1 | Advanced | ~10,000+ words | ~2,000+ | 2,000–4,000 hours |

"Study hours" are rough estimates assuming you're starting from zero. Prior exposure to kanji (through Chinese or Korean), immersion, or related languages affects these significantly.

---

## Test Format

All JLPT levels test three areas:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| 言語知識 (Language Knowledge) | Vocabulary, kanji, grammar |
| 読解 (Reading) | Reading comprehension passages |
| 聴解 (Listening) | Audio tracks with questions |

**No speaking.** No writing. The JLPT is entirely multiple-choice (4 options). This is both its limitation and its accessibility.

**Scoring:** Each section is scored separately. You must pass each section individually — you can't compensate a failed section with a perfect score elsewhere. Sections are curved (scaled scores).

| Level | Total score | Passing score per section |
|---|---|---|
| N5 | 180 | 38 (vocab/grammar), 19 (reading), 19 (listening) |
| N4 | 180 | 38, 19, 19 |
| N3 | 180 | 19, 19, 19 |
| N2 | 180 | 19, 19, 19 |
| N1 | 180 | 19, 19, 19 |

---

## When and Where

**Exam dates:** First Sunday of July / First Sunday of December (most locations)

**Registration:** Opens approximately 3–4 months before the exam. Register at [jlpt.jp](https://www.jlpt.jp) or through your country's local partner organization.

**Locations:** 47 countries/regions. Major cities worldwide. In Japan, dozens of cities.

**Cost:** ~¥5,500–6,000 in Japan. Varies internationally.

---

## What the JLPT Proves

**Officially recognized by:**
- Japanese immigration for visa applications (especially engineer/specialist visas)
- Many Japanese companies for hiring non-Japanese staff
- Some university admissions in Japan
- Professional certification bodies

**N1 specific value:**
- Required or preferred for many professional positions in Japan
- Often a requirement for Japanese government positions
- Demonstrates near-native reading ability

**What it doesn't prove:**
- Speaking ability
- Writing ability
- The ability to function naturally in conversation

Many people with N2 or N1 struggle with everyday conversation — the JLPT doesn't test production. Plan your study to include speaking and writing alongside JLPT prep.

## Study Strategy Principles

### 1. Grammar Before Vocab (at N5–N3)
Grammar structures are finite. Vocabulary is infinite. Learn grammar first — it gives you a scaffold that makes vocabulary stick.

### 2. Spaced Repetition for Vocabulary
Anki or a similar SRS tool is essential for the vocabulary load. N2 requires you to *actively* know 6,000 words — passive recognition isn't enough.

### 3. Past Papers Are the Best Practice
The JLPT has a consistent format. After studying the content, past papers are your best preparation tool. [JLPT Sensei](https://jlptsensei.com) has free resources. Official practice tests are sold at bookstores.

### 4. Reading Every Day
Reading Japanese at your level daily is the single highest-leverage activity for the reading section. Use [NHK Web Easy](../../tracks/news-japanese.md) for N4+.

### 5. Listening Requires Deliberate Practice
The listening section trips up many learners who study only from textbooks. Listen to Japanese content at your target level daily. For N3+, NHK news radio, anime, or drama at appropriate speed.

### 6. Take Full Mock Exams
Time pressure is real. Practice under timed conditions at least 3–4 times before the real exam.

## Choosing Your Level

| If you... | Start at |
|---|---|
| Know nothing | N5 |
| Completed Genki I | N4–N5 |
| Completed Genki II | N3–N4 |
| Have 1+ years of study | Take a practice N3 test and see |
| Read manga comfortably | N3–N2 |
| Watch anime without subtitles | N2–N1 |
| Work or study in Japanese | N2–N1 |

Take a practice test before committing. Don't sandbag (taking N5 when you're clearly N3 level) — it wastes time and money. The JLPT doesn't give "credit" for exceeding the level you tested at.

---

## The Real Goal

The JLPT is a tool, not a destination. Passing N1 with a score of 100/180 while struggling to hold a conversation is less useful than having natural conversations at N3 grammar level with a rich vocabulary.

Use the JLPT to:
- Identify specific weak areas (grammar, vocabulary, reading speed, listening)
- Set concrete milestones
- Get a certificate that matters to Japanese employers and immigration

Don't use the JLPT to:
- Measure your "real" Japanese ability
- Replace actual communication practice

**The best Japanese learners do both:** Study systematically for the JLPT *and* immerse themselves in real Japanese media and conversation.
