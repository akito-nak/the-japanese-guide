# Hiragana — Your First 46 Friends

> *"Hiragana is the gateway. Once you know it, Japan opens up."*

Welcome to the starting line. Hiragana is the foundational phonetic alphabet of Japanese — every sound the language makes can be written in hiragana. Most learners master it in **1–2 weeks** with daily practice. There are no shortcuts, but there are mnemonics, and we've got them.

---

## What Is Hiragana, Exactly?

Japanese has three writing systems (yes, three — breathe):

| System | Use | Count |
|---|---|---|
| **Hiragana** | Native Japanese words, grammar, verb endings | 46 base + variations |
| **Katakana** | Foreign loanwords, emphasis, science terms | 46 base + variations |
| **Kanji** | Meaning-based characters borrowed from Chinese | 2,000+ commonly used |

You'll use hiragana for **everything** at first. Most Japanese learning materials add small hiragana (called **furigana**) above kanji to help beginners read — so knowing hiragana unlocks a lot more than just hiragana words.

## The Full Hiragana Chart

Hiragana is organized by vowel columns and consonant rows. Learn the vowels first — they never change.

### The Five Vowels (and the heart of the system)

| Character | Reading | Sound like... |
|---|---|---|
| あ | a | "ah" (father) |
| い | i | "ee" (feet) |
| う | u | "oo" (but shorter, unrounded lips) |
| え | e | "eh" (bed) |
| お | o | "oh" (open) |

### Main Chart (Gojūon — 五十音)

|  | **a** | **i** | **u** | **e** | **o** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **∅** | あ | い | う | え | お |
| **k** | か | き | く | け | こ |

## Special Characters

### Small っ (Double Consonant)
A small っ before a consonant doubles that consonant — creating a brief pause or "stop":
- きって (kitte) = stamp
- ざっし (zasshi) = magazine
- ちょっと (chotto) = a little

### Long Vowels
In hiragana, long vowels are extended with another vowel character:
- おかあさん (okaasan) = mother — the aa is one long "ah" sound
- おにいさん (oniisan) = older brother
- おおきい (ookii) = big

## Mnemonics to Make Them Stick

Here are memorable associations for the trickiest characters:

| Character | Mnemonic |
|---|---|
| あ | Looks like someone throwing their **A**rms up |
| き | Looks like a **key** |
| さ | A fish on a **sa**fari — the horizontal line is the horizon |
| し | A fishhook — "**sh**e caught a fish" |
| す | Like a swan's **s**winging neck |
| ち | Looks like a 1 with a curve — like a **ch**eek turned sideways |
| つ | A tiny **ts**unami wave |
| ふ | Like a person bowing — **fu**ll bow |
| む | Like a cow's face saying "**moo**" |
| ゆ | Like a fish on a hook — making a "**yu**mmy" face |
| ら | Like a backwards 5 — **ra**cing backwards |
| れ | Like a person with a flowing skirt — **re**dy to dance |
| ろ | Like the number 3 with a loop — **ro**lling around |

> **Pro tip:** Use an app like *Kana Pro* or *Dr. Moku* for visual mnemonics — the pictorial method sticks dramatically faster than rote memorization.

## Stroke Order — Why It Matters

Stroke order isn't just tradition — it makes your handwriting readable and your characters proportional. The general rules:

1. **Top to bottom**
2. **Left to right**
3. **Horizontal before vertical** (in most cases)
4. **Enclosing strokes last**

You don't need to obsess over it for typing, but if you ever want to write Japanese by hand (or pass a handwriting test in Japan), correct stroke order is essential.

## Practice Strategy

### Week 1: Drill Method
- Day 1–2: Vowels + k-row (あいうえお、かきくけこ)
- Day 3–4: s-row + t-row (さしすせそ、たちつてと)
- Day 5–6: n-row + h-row (なにぬねの、はひふへほ)
- Day 7: m-row + y-row + r-row + w-row + ん

### Week 2: Reading Practice
Once you know the characters, **stop using romaji immediately.** Cold turkey. It's uncomfortable for about a week and then everything clicks.

Try reading:
- Hiragana-only children's books (*絵本* — ehon)
- NHK Web Easy (they have furigana on everything)
- Kids' anime with Japanese subtitles

### Recommended Tools
| Tool | Best for |
|---|---|
| Anki (free) | Flashcard drilling with spaced repetition |
| Dr. Moku | Visual mnemonic charts |
| Kana Pro | Quick testing app |
| Tofugu's hiragana guide | Free online with mnemonics |
| Genki I | Textbook that integrates hiragana from chapter 1 |
