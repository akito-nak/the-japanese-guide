# Katakana вҖ” The Angular Cousin

> *"Katakana looks intimidating. Then you realize it says 'hamburger' and you feel great."*

Katakana represents the exact same sounds as hiragana вҖ” but it's used for completely different purposes, and it looks completely different. Angular where hiragana is round. Sharp where hiragana is flowing. Think of hiragana as cursive and katakana as print.

---

## When Is Katakana Used?

| Context | Example |
|---|---|
| **Foreign loanwords** | гӮігғјгғ’гғј (kЕҚhД«) = coffee |
| **Foreign names** | гӮўгғЎгғӘгӮ« (Amerika) = America |
| **Emphasis / impact** | Like using *italics* or **bold** in English |
| **Scientific terms** | Plant/animal names in biology |
| **Onomatopoeia** | гғүгӮӯгғүгӮӯ (dokidoki) = heart pounding |
| **Slang & internet speak** | гӮ¬гғҒ (gachi) = seriously/for real |
| **Menus in foreign restaurants** | гғ”гӮ¶, гғ‘гӮ№гӮҝ, гӮ«гғ¬гғј |
| **Brand names** | гӮҪгғӢгғј (Sony), гғҲгғЁгӮҝ (Toyota) |

Learning katakana also unlocks a **huge** vocabulary shortcut: thousands of English words have been absorbed into Japanese. Once you recognize them, you have an instant head start.

## The Long Vowel Mark: гғј

In katakana, long vowels are written with a dash вҖ” **гғј** вҖ” instead of repeating the vowel. This is one of the most common characters you'll see on signs and menus.

## Confusing Pairs вҖ” The Katakana Traps

Some characters look dangerously similar. These are the ones that trip everyone up:

| Pair | Characters | How to tell apart |
|---|---|---|
| гӮў vs гӮў | гӮў = small hat shape | The cross piece angles differently |
| гӮҪ vs гғі | гӮҪ (so) vs гғі (n) | гӮҪ leans right, гғі leans left вҖ” *so* vs *n* |
| гӮ· vs гғ„ | гӮ· (shi) vs гғ„ (tsu) | гӮ· has dots stacked left, гғ„ has dots stacked right |
| гӮҰ vs гғІ | гӮҰ (u) vs гғІ (wo) | гғІ is almost never used in modern Japanese |
| гғҺ vs гӮҪ | гғҺ (no) vs гӮҪ (so) | гғҺ is a single stroke; гӮҪ has two extra marks |
| гғӯ vs еҸЈ | гғӯ = katakana ro | еҸЈ = kanji for "mouth" вҖ” they look identical! Context tells you |

> **Memory trick for гӮҪ vs гӮ·:** Think of гӮҪ as a person (гӮҪ) **so**aring to the right. гӮ· faces left, like it's **sh**y.

## Katakana in Pop Culture

Katakana shows up *everywhere* in manga, anime, and games вҖ” often for stylistic effect:

- **Villains and robots** often speak in katakana to sound mechanical or otherworldly
- **Impact sounds** are in katakana: гғүгӮ«гғі (dokan = boom), гғҗгғі (ban = bang)
- **Power-up names** in anime: гӮ№гғјгғ‘гғјгӮөгӮӨгғӨдәә (Super Saiyan)
- **Foreign characters** in stories speak in katakana to signal their accent

## Stroke Order вҖ” Katakana

The stroke order rules from hiragana apply equally here: top to bottom, left to right, horizontal before vertical, no lifting the pen mid-stroke. What changes is the *character of the strokes* вҖ” katakana is angular and architectural where hiragana is curved and flowing. Most katakana characters have **fewer strokes** (1вҖ“3) and **sharper direction changes** than their hiragana equivalents.

---

### Why Katakana Stroke Order Especially Matters

The confusing pairs (гӮҪ/гғі, гӮ·/гғ„, гғҺ/гӮҪ) are *only* distinguishable by stroke angle and direction. Knowing the correct stroke order makes these visually distinct:

| Pair | How stroke order reveals the difference |
|---|---|
| **гӮ· (shi) vs гғ„ (tsu)** | гӮ·: two dots go вҶҷ left-to-right, main stroke goes вҶ— upward. гғ„: two dots go вҶҳ right-to-left, main stroke goes вҶ“ downward |
| **гӮҪ (so) vs гғі (n)** | гӮҪ: short stroke goes вҶҳ then long stroke sweeps вҶҳвҶ—. гғі: short stroke goes вҶ— then long stroke sweeps вҶҷ |
| **гғҺ (no) vs гӮҪ (so)** | гғҺ is **1 stroke** going вҶҳ. гӮҪ is **2 strokes**: a short mark then a sweep |

Memorizing these as stroke-sequence patterns is more reliable than memorizing them as pictures.

### Vowels (гӮўиЎҢ)

**гӮў (a) вҖ” 2 strokes**

```
   в‘ в”Җ вҶҳ
   в‘Ўв”Җ вҶ“
```
в‘  Diagonal going upper-left to lower-right  
в‘Ў Horizontal right, then turns sharply вҶ“  

**гӮӨ (i) вҖ” 2 strokes**
в‘  Short diagonal вҶҷ  
в‘Ў Longer diagonal вҶҷ (taller, parallel, further right)  

**гӮҰ (u) вҖ” 3 strokes**
в‘  Short horizontal вҶ’ at top  
в‘Ў Left vertical вҶ“  
в‘ў Right vertical вҶ“ (the two verticals are joined by в‘ )  

**гӮЁ (e) вҖ” 3 strokes**
в‘  Horizontal вҶ’ (top)  
в‘Ў Vertical вҶ“ (center)  
в‘ў Horizontal вҶ’ (bottom)  

**гӮӘ (o) вҖ” 3 strokes**
в‘  Horizontal вҶ’  
в‘Ў Vertical вҶ“ (crosses в‘ )  
в‘ў Diagonal вҶҷ starting from right of в‘

### S-Row (гӮөиЎҢ)

| Char | Count | Key notes |
|---|---|---|
| гӮө (sa) | 3 | в‘ вҶ’ в‘ЎвҶ’ в‘ўвҶ“ crossing both (resembles a table) |
| гӮ· (shi) | 3 | в‘ вҶҷ в‘ЎвҶҷ (both dots go down-left) в‘ўвҶ— long stroke going up-right |
| гӮ№ (su) | 2 | в‘ вҶ’вҶҷ (top, curves down-left) в‘ЎвҶҷ small hook at bottom |
| гӮ» (se) | 2 | в‘ вҶ’вҶ“ (top and right) в‘ЎвҶ’ (long horizontal left through в‘ ) |
| гӮҪ (so) | 2 | в‘ вҶҳ short diagonal down-right в‘ЎвҶҳвҶ— long sweep down then up-right |

> **гӮ· vs гғ„ visual key:** Tilt гӮ· and it reads like 'shi' in English вҖ” the three strokes angle *upward* to the right. Tilt гғ„ and it reads like 'tsu' вҖ” the strokes angle *downward*.

## Practice Strategy

Because katakana represents the same sounds as hiragana, you already know the phonetics вҖ” you just need to recognize new shapes. This makes katakana **faster to learn** for most people.

### The Loanword Method (Fastest)
1. Learn the characters using mnemonics
2. Start reading katakana menus, signs, and labels immediately
3. Guess the English word вҖ” you'll be right surprisingly often
4. Keep a running list of false friends (they're fun)

### Recommended Practice
- **Read menus** at restaurants (almost entirely katakana)
- **Watch anime with Japanese subtitles** вҖ” sound effects are katakana
- **Scan train maps** вҖ” many station names have katakana nearby
- **Play Japanese games** вҖ” item names and proper nouns are usually katakana
