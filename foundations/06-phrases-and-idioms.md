# Phrases & Idioms — Sound Like You Know What You're Doing

> *"The difference between a textbook learner and someone who sounds natural is usually a handful of idioms and phrases that native speakers use without thinking."*

Japanese is full of expressions that can't be translated word for word — and many of them are used so constantly that skipping them means missing half of what's happening around you.

## Social Lubricant — Phrases Japanese People Say All the Time

These are the phrases that fill conversation. Use them and you'll immediately sound more natural:

| Phrase | Reading | When to use it |
|---|---|---|
| お疲れ様です | otsukaresama desu | "Good work" — said to coworkers constantly, when leaving for the day, when someone finishes something |
| ご苦労様です | gokurōsama desu | "Thank you for your hard work" — said *downward* to staff/subordinates only |
| よろしくお願いします | yoroshiku onegaishimasu | "Please treat me well / I'm counting on you" — essential for introductions, requests, sign-offs in emails |
| お世話になっております | osewa ni natte orimasu | "Thank you for your continued support" — how almost every business email/call starts |
| なるほど | naruhodo | "I see / That makes sense" — shows you're following along |
| そうですね | sō desu ne | "Indeed / Is that so?" — thoughtful agreement, buying thinking time |
| ちょっと… | chotto... | "Hmm, that's a bit..." — polite, vague refusal. Japanese people rarely say a flat "no." |
| 大丈夫です | daijōbu desu | "It's fine / I'm okay" — can mean yes OR no depending on context! |
| 頑張ってください | ganbatte kudasai | "Do your best / Good luck" — said before exams, events, challenges |
| 頑張ります | ganbarimasu | "I'll do my best" — the response |
| お邪魔します | ojama shimasu | "Excuse the intrusion" — said when entering someone's home |
| ただいま / おかえり | tadaima / okaeri | "I'm home" / "Welcome back" — the pair that every household uses |
| いってきます / いってらっしゃい | ittekimasu / itterasshai | "I'm heading out" / "Have a good day" — the other essential household pair |

## Proverbs (ことわざ)

Shorter proverbs used in everyday speech:

| Proverb | Reading | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 案ずるより産むが易し | anzuru yori umu ga yasushi | The act of doing is easier than the worry about it |
| 急がば回れ | isogaba maware | If you're in a hurry, take the long way round — shortcuts backfire |
| 笑う門には福来る | warau kado ni wa fuku kitaru | Happiness comes to those who smile / Laughter brings good fortune |
| 知らぬが仏 | shiranu ga hotoke | Ignorance is bliss (literally: not knowing is like being Buddha) |
| 出る杭は打たれる | deru kui wa utareru | The nail that sticks out gets hammered in — don't stand out too much |
| 論より証拠 | ron yori shōko | Evidence over argument — show, don't tell |
| 継続は力なり | keizoku wa chikara nari | Continuity is power — consistency wins |

> **出る杭は打たれる** is essential cultural knowledge — it explains a lot about Japanese social dynamics, workplace behavior, and why standing out can be uncomfortable in Japan.

## Context-Dependent Phrases

Japanese has many phrases that change meaning entirely based on delivery and situation:

### 大丈夫 (daijōbu) — The Wildcard
- "大丈夫ですか？" → "Are you okay?"
- "大丈夫です。" → Could mean "I'm fine" OR "No thank you" OR "It's alright, don't worry"
- Context is everything. Watch for which question came before it.

### ちょっと (chotto) — The Soft Refusal
- Direct: "Just a minute" / "A little bit"
- Social: "That's... a bit..." (the trailing off means *no*, and pressing further is rude)

### なんか / なんて (nanka / nante) — Softeners
Used to soften statements, express mild self-deprecation, or add vagueness:
- なんか面白い — "It's sort of interesting"
- 私なんて… — "Someone like me..." (self-deprecating)

### よろしく — The Phrase That Does Everything
In emails, meetings, introductions, requests, and farewells:
- At an introduction: "I look forward to working with you"
- In an email sign-off: "Best regards / Thanks in advance"
- After handing off a task: "I'm counting on you"
- Alone as a casual reply to thanks: "No worries"

## Useful Filler Words (and Why They Matter)

Fillers show active listening and keep conversation flowing:

| Filler | Meaning/Use |
|---|---|
| えーと / あの | Um... / Uh... (thinking pause) |
| そうですね... | "Let me see..." (politely buying time) |
| なるほど | "I see" / "That makes sense" |
| うん | Yeah (casual agreement) |
| へー！ | "Oh really!" (surprise/interest) |
| まあね | "Well, sort of / I guess" |
| 確かに | "Certainly / That's true" |
| それはそうと | "By the way" (topic change) |

Japanese conversation involves a lot of **あいづち** (aizuchi) — back-channel responses that show you're listening. Nodding and saying うん、なるほど、そうですね while someone speaks is expected and polite. Silent listening can feel disengaged to Japanese speakers.

## Phrases to Learn by Level

### Beginner
すみません。ここはどこですか？
*(Sumimasen. Koko wa doko desu ka?)*
Excuse me, where is this?

### Intermediate
お時間があればお話ししたいのですが。
*(Ojikan ga areba ohanashi shitai no desu ga.)*
If you have time, I'd like to have a talk.

### Advanced
ご指摘の点、真摯に受け止めております。
*(Goshiteki no ten, shinshi ni uketomete orimasu.)*
I take your feedback very seriously. (Business/formal apology)
