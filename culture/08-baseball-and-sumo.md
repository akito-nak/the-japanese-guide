# Baseball & Sumo — Japan's Two National Pastimes

> *"Sumo is 1,500 years old and rooted in Shinto ritual. Baseball arrived in 1872 and became a national obsession within a generation. That Japan made both its own — completely, unapologetically — tells you everything about how this country absorbs and transforms what it loves."*

Japan takes sports seriously. Not just as entertainment, but as expression: of discipline, of identity, of regional pride, of the values that matter. These two sports couldn't be more different in origin, but they've both come to carry enormous cultural weight in contemporary Japan.

---

## ⚾ 野球 — Yakyū (Baseball)

### How Baseball Became Japanese

Baseball arrived in Japan in **1872**, introduced by American teacher Horace Wilson at what is now Tokyo University. Within two decades it had spread to schools and universities across the country. Within a generation, it was no longer "an American sport" — it was *Japanese*.

The reason baseball took root so deeply is cultural alignment: the game rewards **patience, precision, group discipline, and individual excellence within a team structure** — values that mapped almost perfectly onto the Japanese aesthetic. A pitcher's psychological duel with a batter. The geometry of fielding. The patience of waiting for the right pitch. It felt familiar even when it was new.

By the 1930s, Japan was already producing world-class players. By the 1960s, professional baseball (プロ野球 — puroyakyū) had become the country's dominant spectator sport. It has never fully relinquished that position.

### 安部磯雄 — Abe Isoo: The Father of Japanese Baseball

If Horace Wilson brought the ball and the bat, **[安部磯雄 (Abe Isoo, 1865–1949)](https://abe-isoo.vercel.app/en)** gave the game its soul.

Born in Fukuoka to a family of the declining samurai class, Abe was educated at **同志社大学 (Dōshisha University)** before traveling to the United States in 1891 to study at Hartford Theological Seminary in Connecticut. He spent four transformative years in America — years that would shape everything that came after. He returned to Japan in 1895 carrying three things that seem almost impossible to reconcile: a deep Christian faith, a commitment to socialist reform, and an evangelical passion for baseball.

He joined **早稲田大学 (Waseda University)** as a professor and promptly introduced baseball to the campus. But Abe's approach was never just about the sport. He saw baseball as a **moral and educational instrument** — a way to build character, cultivate discipline, and foster the kind of cooperative individualism he believed modern Japan needed. Where other educators saw athletics as a distraction, Abe saw it as a curriculum.

In **1905**, he organized **Japan's first collegiate baseball tournament** — a landmark moment that planted the seed for the structured, deeply competitive university and high school baseball culture that defines the sport in Japan today. The obsession with Kōshien, the emotional weight carried by school baseball, the idea that playing the game properly is as important as winning it — these trace a direct line back to Abe's foundational philosophy.

What makes Abe Isoo so remarkable is the coherence of his contradictions. He was a **Christian pacifist** who corresponded with Leo Tolstoy on questions of peace, non-violence, and social reform — a genuinely extraordinary intellectual exchange across two cultures. He was a **socialist politician** who founded the Socialist Democratic Party and championed workers' rights at a time when doing so carried real personal risk. And he was a **baseball evangelist** who believed sport could quietly transform a society that lectures and pamphlets could not reach.

He understood something that took the rest of the world decades to articulate: that how a nation plays says as much about its values as how it governs. The patience, the respect between opponents, the emphasis on form and process alongside outcome — Abe encoded these into Japanese baseball from the beginning.

His influence is mostly invisible now, the way the foundations of a building are invisible. But walk into Kōshien during the Summer tournament, watch a team bow to the field after losing, watch players scoop up the infield dirt to take home, and you are standing inside something Abe Isoo built.

> 「野球は人格を鍛える最良の手段の一つだ。」
> "Baseball is one of the finest means of forging human character."
> — Abe Isoo

### 読売ジャイアンツ vs 阪神タイガース — The Greatest Rivalry

The **巨人 (Kyojin — Giants) vs 阪神 (Hanshin Tigers)** rivalry is the Yankees–Red Sox of Japan, except the cultural stakes may be even higher because it maps directly onto the Tokyo vs. Osaka cultural divide.

- **Giants:** Based in Tokyo, backed by the Yomiuri newspaper empire. The most decorated team in Japanese history (21 Japan Series titles). Many outside Tokyo consider rooting for them an act of cultural submission.
- **Tigers:** Based in the old Koshien Stadium in Nishinomiya (between Osaka and Kobe). Passionate, regional, and defined by their fans rather than their trophy case. Tigers fans are legendary for their intensity.

When the Tigers won the Japan Series in **2023** — their first championship in 38 years — Osaka erupted. Fans jumped into the 道頓堀 (Dōtonbori) canal in a tradition so established that the city prepared for it with safety measures.

### 甲子園 — Kōshien: High School Baseball's Cathedral

**甲子園球場 (Kōshien Kyūjō)** in Nishinomiya is one of the most emotionally significant sports venues in Japan. Twice a year it hosts:

- **選抜高等学校野球大会 (Spring Kōshien):** March/April — invitation-based tournament
- **全国高等学校野球選手権大会 (Summer Kōshien):** August — the national championship

High school baseball at Kōshien is not just a tournament. It's a **cultural institution.** NHK broadcasts every game. The nation watches. Teams from small rural schools compete against urban powerhouses. When teams lose, they scoop up infield dirt to take home as a keepsake (土を持ち帰る — tsuchi wo mochikaeru). Players cry openly. Coaches cry. The crowd cries.

The pitcher who dominates a Summer Kōshien becomes a national hero overnight. This is where careers are made, dreams are set, and the entire country witnesses it together.

> 「甲子園に行きたい」
> "I want to go to Kōshien."
> — The dream of every high school baseball player in Japan.

**高校野球 (kōkō yakyū — high school baseball)** has its own deeply embedded culture:
- Teams travel in matching uniforms even to watch other games
- Players shave their heads (丸刈り — marugari) as a sign of commitment
- Exhausted pitchers throwing complete games despite injury — and the debate this sparks about player welfare
- The song 「栄冠は君に輝く」(Eikin wa Kimi ni Kagayaku) played at the championship

### 日本の野球とメジャーリーグ — Japan and the Majors

Japan has exported baseball talent to MLB for decades, but the modern era is something different entirely:

**野茂英雄 (Nomo Hideo, 1968–):** The first to truly break through in MLB (Dodgers, 1995). His "tornado" delivery and immediate dominance shocked America and opened the door permanently.

**イチロー (Ichiro Suzuki, 1973–):** Redefined what a Japanese player could be in MLB. 3,089 MLB hits. 10 Gold Gloves. A style of play — precise, disciplined, relentless — that looked like a different game from what Americans were used to. He didn't adapt to American baseball; American baseball had to make room for him.

**松井秀喜 (Matsui Hideki, 1974–):** "Godzilla." Giants legend who became a World Series MVP with the Yankees (2009).

**大谷翔平 (Ohtani Shōhei, 1994–):** The phenomenon. A two-way player (投手/打者 — pitcher/hitter) at elite level simultaneously — something that hadn't been done sustainably since Babe Ruth. His 2021 season (9 wins as pitcher, 46 home runs as hitter) was statistically unprecedented. His $700M contract with the Dodgers in 2023 was the largest in professional sports history. In Japan, his games air at 6am and people set alarms. He is genuinely the most famous person in the country.

### Baseball Fan Culture

Japanese baseball fans are among the most organized in the world:

- **応援団 (ōendan):** Cheer squads in the outfield bleachers with trumpets, drums, coordinated chants for every player. The sound level is extraordinary.
- **選手別応援歌 (player-specific cheer songs):** Each player has their own theme song. When they come up to bat, the crowd sings it in unison.
- **傘 (kasa — umbrellas):** Yakult Swallows fans open umbrellas in unison after home runs.
- **応援グッズ (cheer goods):** Plastic inflatable sticks (風船 — fūsen), megaphones, happi coats in team colors.
- **甲子園の六甲おろし:** The Tigers' anthem — Kōshien shakes when 40,000 people sing it.

## 相撲 — Sumō

### The Ancient Sport

Sumo is not simply a sport. It is a **神事 (shinji)** — a Shinto ritual — that has been practiced for at least 1,500 years. According to the Kojiki (712 CE), the fate of the Japanese islands was decided by a sumo bout between gods. The sport is wrapped in ritual, symbolism, and ceremony that makes it unlike any other athletic competition on earth.

Every element of sumo has meaning:
- The **土俵 (dohyō — ring)** is a sacred space, constructed by a Shinto carpenter, purified with salt
- The roof above the dohyō at the major tournaments resembles a **Shinto shrine roof**
- The referee (行司 — gyōji) carries a short sword — symbolically, he is prepared to commit ritual suicide if he makes a wrong call
- Wrestlers throw **salt (塩)** to purify the ring before the bout
- The **横綱 (yokozuna)** performs a rope dance (土俵入り — dohyōiri) derived from Shinto ritual

---

### The Ranking System — 番付 (Banzuke)

Sumo's hierarchical ranking system is one of the most rigorous in professional sport. Rankings are published on the 番付表 (banzuke-hyō — ranking sheet) before each tournament:

| Rank | Japanese | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 横綱 | Yokozuna | Grand champion — the pinnacle. Rarely more than 3–4 active at once. Can never be demoted — must retire if performance falls. |
| 大関 | Ōzeki | Champion — second highest. Demoted if poor results sustained. |
| 関脇 | Sekiwake | Third rank |
| 小結 | Komusubi | Fourth rank |
| 前頭 | Maegashira | Upper division (numbered 1–17, lower number = higher rank) |
| 幕下 | Makushita | Second division — not paid professionals |
| 三段目 | Sandanme | Third division |
| 序二段 | Jonidan | Fourth division |
| 序ノ口 | Jonokuchi | Entry level |

**幕内 (Makuuchi)** — the top division (横綱 through 前頭) — is what you watch at major tournaments. Only ~42 wrestlers compete here.

**昇格 / 降格 (shōkaku / kōkaku):** Promotion and demotion happen every tournament based on 勝ち越し (kachikoshi — winning majority) or 負け越し (makekoshi — losing majority) records.

### A Bout: What's Actually Happening

A sumo match (一番 — hitotsu) is often over in seconds. But everything before the first charge (立ち合い — tachi-ai) is theater:

1. **入場 (nyūjō):** Wrestlers enter to their own theme music, wearing elaborate decorated aprons (化粧廻し — keshōmawashi)
2. **四股 (shiko):** The iconic leg-stamping — purifying the ring, driving evil spirits into the earth
3. **塩まき (shio maki):** Throwing salt to purify the ring
4. **仕切り (shikiri):** The wrestlers crouch, stare each other down, and rise — repeatedly — until they're ready. Top division wrestlers get up to 4 minutes for this psychological duel.
5. **立ち合い (tachi-ai):** The simultaneous charge — the most important moment. A false start (フライング) is called back.
6. **取組 (torikumi):** The bout itself

**Winning conditions:** You win by either:
- Pushing/forcing your opponent out of the ring (押し出し — oshidashi)
- Making any part of their body other than the foot soles touch the ground (down = loss)

There are no weight classes, no time limits, no rounds.

### Legendary Yokozuna

The title of **横綱 (Yokozuna)** is the most prestigious in Japanese sport. Only 73 have ever been awarded it. Each defined an era:

**双葉山定次 (Futabayama, 1912–1968) — 69th consecutive wins**
The record that may never be broken. Yokozuna Futabayama won 69 consecutive bouts between 1936–1943. In defeat, he reportedly sent a telegram reading: 「未だ木鶏たりえず」— "I have not yet achieved the state of the wooden rooster" (a Zhuangzi reference — the highest state of calm mastery). That quote is quoted by sumo fans 80 years later.

**千代の富士貢 (Chiyonofuji, 1955–2016) — "The Wolf"**
Perhaps the most popular yokozuna of the modern era. Lean and muscular in a sport of giants, he dominated the 1980s through intelligence and technique over raw mass. His retirement announcement press conference — where he said simply 「体力の限界です。引退します」("I have reached my physical limit. I will retire.") — was watched live by millions and remains one of the most quoted moments in Japanese sports history.

**曙太郎 (Akebono, 1969–2024) — First foreign-born Yokozuna**
The American-born Hawaiian who became the first non-Japanese yokozuna in 1993. His promotion sparked intense national debate about tradition and identity — the Japan Sumo Association ultimately concluded that sumo is universal, not ethnic. Akebono's achievement opened the door for the Mongolian era that followed.

**朝青龍明徳 (Asashōryū, 1980–) and 白鵬翔 (Hakuhō, 1985–) — The Mongolian Dominance**
From the mid-2000s through the 2020s, Mongolian wrestlers — primarily Asashōryū and Hakuhō — dominated sumo completely. Hakuhō's 45 tournament championships is a record that obliterates all prior benchmarks. This dominance triggered complicated national conversations about the sport's identity, with Japanese fans simultaneously proud of sumo's global appeal and longing for a Japanese champion.

**照ノ富士春雄 (Terunofuji, 1991–) — The Comeback King**
Mongolian-born, reached ōzeki in 2015, then collapsed to the lowest division due to catastrophic knee injuries. Most assumed his career was over. He rebuilt himself, won his way back through every single division, and became yokozuna in 2021. His story is the most extraordinary sporting comeback in recent Japanese memory.

### Sumo and Contemporary Japan

**The "Japanese yokozuna problem":** Japan has not had a Japanese-born yokozuna since **稀勢の里寛 (Kisenosato, 1986–)**, who was promoted in 2017 and retired in 2019 after injury. The national longing for a Japanese champion is acute — stadium attendance spikes dramatically when a Japanese wrestler performs well in a tournament.

**国技 (kokugi — national sport):** Sumo holds this distinction. No other sport does. But what "national" means when the top performers are often Mongolian, Georgian, Bulgarian, or Hawaiian has been a live cultural question for two decades.

**Sumo's appeal to young Japanese:** TV ratings for sumo have struggled among younger demographics. The Japan Sumo Association has responded with social media, YouTube highlights, and promoting charismatic younger wrestlers. The sport is genuinely trying to evolve while preserving its ritual soul.

**Foreign interest:** Sumo tourism has grown rapidly. The "sumo experience" (相撲体験 — sumō taiken) at stable morning practices (朝稽古見学 — asa-geiko kengaku) has become popular with international visitors. Some stables allow observation of morning practice — a genuinely extraordinary thing to witness.

### Watching Sumo: What to Know

**Attending a tournament:**
- **枡席 (masuseki):** Box seats, tatami-floored, 4 per box — shared with strangers if you buy individual seats
- **椅子席 (isu-seki):** Chair seats, more conventional
- **立ち見 (tachimi):** Standing room — available on the day, cheapest option
- Morning bouts feature lower-division wrestlers. Top division starts late afternoon.
- Arrive by 3:30pm to see the full upper-division program
- The 千秋楽 (senshūraku — final day) has the most drama and longest ceremony

**Watching on TV:**
- NHK broadcasts all six tournaments live from 3:00pm daily
- **NHK BS** often shows additional coverage
- The NHK sumo broadcast team is an institution — their calm, knowledgeable commentary is itself part of the experience

**Essential viewing moments:**
- 横綱土俵入り (yokozuna dohyōiri) — the grand champion's ring entrance ceremony. If a yokozuna is active, do not miss this.
- 千秋楽の優勝決定戦 — championship playoff or deciding final-day bout
- The **懸賞金 (kenshōkin)** parade — advertisers sponsor prizes; the yobidashi walks the ring holding banners before a featured bout

### Baseball and Sumo Together: What They Mean to Japan

The two sports occupy different emotional territories:

**Baseball** is Japan as it chose to become — the Meiji embrace of the foreign, the transformation of something external into something deeply national. It's regional pride (阪神 vs 巨人), youth and possibility (Kōshien), and now global validation (Ohtani).

**Sumo** is Japan as it has always been — ancient, ritualized, rooted in Shinto, carrying the weight of history in every salt throw. It asks what "Japanese" means and doesn't always give a comfortable answer.

Together, they hold the country's sporting identity between them: one looking outward at the world, one looking inward at what makes Japan Japan.

> 「野球は国民的娯楽、相撲は国技。どちらも日本の心です。」
> "Baseball is the national pastime; sumo is the national sport. Both are the soul of Japan."
