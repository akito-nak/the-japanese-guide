# Casual Japanese — How Friends Actually Talk

> *"Textbook Japanese is a suit. Casual Japanese is what you actually wear. You'll spend most of your life in one of them."*

Casual Japanese (タメ語 — tamego, or くだけた話し方) is what Japanese people speak with close friends, family, and anyone they're genuinely comfortable around. It's fast, contracted, particle-light, and often doesn't bother finishing sentences.

Mastering it takes time — but even recognizing it will transform your comprehension of anime, drama, and real conversation.

---

## The Core Shifts from Polite to Casual

### 1. Drop the ます / です

| Polite | Casual |
|---|---|
| 行きます | 行く |
| 食べます | 食べる |
| 来ます | 来る |
| します | する |
| そうです | そう / そうだ |
| 大丈夫です | 大丈夫 / 大丈夫だ |
| 分かりません | 分からない / 分かんない |

### 2. Drop Particles

| Full sentence | Casual version |
|---|---|
| 学校に行く | 学校行く |
| これを食べる | これ食べる |
| 明日は暇ですか？ | 明日暇？ |
| どこへ行くの？ | どこ行くの？ |

### 3. Contract Verbs

| Polite/Plain | Casual contracted | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 〜ている | 〜てる | ongoing action |
| 〜ていた | 〜てた | was doing |
| 〜てしまう | 〜ちゃう | end up doing |
| 〜てしまった | 〜ちゃった | ended up doing |
| 〜ておく | 〜とく | do in advance |
| 〜てしまえ | 〜ちゃえ | just go ahead and do it |
| 〜なければいけない | 〜なきゃ / 〜なくちゃ | have to do |
| 〜なければならない | 〜なきゃいけない | have to do |

## Sentence Endings That Carry Meaning

Sentence-final particles and endings shape the entire tone of casual speech:

| Ending | Gender lean | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 〜ね | Neutral | Seeking agreement / "right?" |
| 〜よ | Neutral | Asserting, informing — "you know" |
| 〜ぞ | Male | Strong assertion — "I'm telling you" |
| 〜ぜ | Male | Casual assertion / emphasis |
| 〜わ | Female | Soft emphasis (Kansai also uses for strong assertion) |
| 〜の | Neutral (often female) | Seeking explanation / softener |
| 〜な | Male / Kansai | Agreement / thinking out loud |
| 〜かな | Neutral | "I wonder..." |
| 〜じゃん | Neutral (youth) | "Isn't it? / Clearly, right?" |
| 〜もん / 〜もの | Female / child-like | "Because..." (pouting/justifying) |
| 〜っけ | Neutral | Trying to remember: "wasn't it...?" |
| 〜よな / 〜だよな | Male | Strong agreement-seeking |
| 〜だっけ？ | Neutral | "Was it...? / Remind me..." |

## Common Casual Expressions

### Affirmatives
| Expression | Meaning |
|---|---|
| うん | Yeah |
| そうそう | Yeah exactly / That's right |
| だよね！ | Right?! Exactly! |
| まじで | Seriously? / For real |
|確かに | That's true / Fair point |
| ほんとに？ | Really? |
| そうなんだ | Oh is that so |
| あー、なるほど | Ah, I see |

### Negatives / Refusals
| Expression | Meaning |
|---|---|
| ちょっと... | That's a bit... (soft no) |
| むり | No way / impossible |
| やだ / いやだ | No / I don't want to |
| どうかな... | I'm not sure... |
| うーん... | Hmm... (considering, maybe no) |

## Casual Questions

Questions in casual speech don't need か — intonation does the work:

| Casual question | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 行く？ | You going? |
| 何してる？ | What are you doing? / What are you up to? |
| どうした？ | What happened? / You okay? |
| 大丈夫？ | You alright? |
| どうだった？ | How was it? |
| 何食べたい？ | What do you want to eat? |
| 一緒に行かない？ | Want to come along? (negative question = invitation) |
| これ知ってる？ | You know this? / Have you seen this? |

## The Art of the Incomplete Sentence

Japanese allows sentences to trail off with just a particle, leaving the meaning implied:

| Said | Unsaid | Full meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ちょっと... | 難しいです | That's a bit... [difficult] |
| 行けたら... | 行きます | If I can make it... [I'll go] |
| それって... | 変じゃない？ | That's kind of... [weird, isn't it?] |
| 実は... | 言いにくいんだけど | The thing is... [this is hard to say] |
| でも... | | But... [I don't agree / there's a problem] |

Trailing off is a social skill in Japanese. It lets you say something without committing to it — giving the listener room to offer an out. Learning to read the implied meaning is part of learning the language.

## How to Invite, Accept, and Decline Casually

### Inviting
> 今夜飲みに行かない？
> (Tonight, want to go for drinks? — negative question = casual invitation)

> 明日ヒマ？一緒にどこか行こうよ。
> (Free tomorrow? Let's go somewhere together.)

### Accepting
> いいね！行こう！
> (Sounds good! Let's go!)

> まじ？行く行く！
> (Really? Definitely coming!)

### Declining (the art of the soft no)
> ちょっとその日は...
> (That day is a bit... [busy/not great])

> 今日はちょっと体調が悪くて...
> (Today I'm feeling a bit under the weather...)

> 次回ぜひ！
> (Next time for sure!)

The key: you almost never say a flat "no" directly in Japanese. Give a vague excuse and trail off. The listener understands.

## Casual Japanese by Context

### With Close Friends (砕けた)
> A: ねえ、今日何する？
> B: うーん、別に何も。映画でも見る？
> A: いいじゃん！何がいい？
> B: ホラーとか見たくない？
> A: え〜、怖いの苦手だよ。
> B: じゃ、アクションにしよ。

*(Hey, what are you doing today? / Hmm, nothing in particular. Watch a movie? / Sounds good! What do you want to see? / Don't you want to watch horror? / Eh~, I'm bad with scary stuff. / Then let's go with action.)*

### With Younger Siblings / Kids
Casual but often instructive:
> ご飯食べたの？
> (Did you eat?)
> もうお風呂入りなよ。
> (Go take a bath already.)

### SNS / Chat
Maximum abbreviation and minimal politeness with peers:
> おはよ〜！今日学校？
> (Morning~! School today?)
> 無理、眠すぎ
> (Can't, too sleepy)

## Things to Watch Out For

### Casual Can Flip to Rude Fast
The same sentence can be rude or friendly based on tone and relationship:
> 何してんの？ (What are you doing?) — affectionate with a friend, accusatory to a stranger

### Don't Be Too Casual Too Soon
In Japan, relationships typically require a "warming period" before casual speech is appropriate. Using casual speech with someone you just met can come across as arrogant or disrespectful — especially with older people.

**General rule:** Follow the other person's lead. If they shift to casual, you can follow. Don't initiate the shift unless you're clearly both on the same level.

### あの... and えっと...
These are absolutely normal filler words. Using them makes you sound more natural, not less confident.

## Casual Speech for Different Ages

Japan's casual speech isn't monolithic — teenagers, young adults, middle-aged people, and elderly people all use different casual vocabulary:

| Age group | Distinctive casual features |
|---|---|
| Teens | Heavy use of slang, ヤバい, きつい, エモい, ぴえん |
| 20s–30s | Moderate slang, LINE-influenced, some 2ch vocabulary |
| 40s–50s | More traditional casual forms, fewer recent slang |
| 60s+ | Regional dialect features, older vocabulary, もの/のだ patterns |
