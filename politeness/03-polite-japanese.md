# Polite Japanese — The Everyday Standard

> *"です/ます form is like saying 'please' and 'thank you.' It's the baseline of civilized interaction with anyone who isn't your best friend."*

Polite Japanese (丁寧語 — teinego, or ですます体) is the default register for almost all public-facing interaction in Japan. It's what shop staff use with you, what you use with strangers, what coworkers use when they first meet, and what textbooks spend most of their time teaching.

If casual Japanese is the t-shirt, polite Japanese is the business casual — appropriate nearly everywhere, never wrong.

---

## The Core Forms

### Verbs: The ます Form

The polite present/future is made by replacing the verb's plain form ending with ます:

| Plain form | ます form | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 行く (iku) | 行きます (ikimasu) | go |
| 食べる (taberu) | 食べます (tabemasu) | eat |
| 飲む (nomu) | 飲みます (nomimasu) | drink |
| 来る (kuru) | 来ます (kimasu) | come |
| する (suru) | します (shimasu) | do |
| 話す (hanasu) | 話します (hanashimasu) | speak |
| 見る (miru) | 見ます (mimasu) | see |
| 読む (yomu) | 読みます (yomimasu) | read |
| 書く (kaku) | 書きます (kakimasu) | write |

### Full Conjugation Table (ます Form)

| Tense / Mood | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present/future positive | 〜ます | 食べます |
| Present/future negative | 〜ません | 食べません |
| Past positive | 〜ました | 食べました |
| Past negative | 〜ませんでした | 食べませんでした |
| Volitional ("let's") | 〜ましょう | 食べましょう |
| Invitation / suggestion | 〜ませんか？ | 食べませんか？ (Would you like to eat?) |
| Request | 〜てください | 食べてください |

### The Copula: です

| Form | Japanese |
|---|---|
| Present positive | です |
| Present negative | じゃないです / ではありません (more formal) |
| Past positive | でした |
| Past negative | じゃなかったです / ではありませんでした |

## Standard Polite Phrases for Every Situation

### Introductions
> はじめまして。田中と申します。どうぞよろしくお願いします。
> (Hajimemashite. Tanaka to mōshimasu. Dōzo yoroshiku onegaishimasu.)
> Nice to meet you. My name is Tanaka. I look forward to getting to know you.

> こちらこそよろしくお願いします。
> (Kochira koso yoroshiku onegaishimasu.)
> Likewise, the pleasure is mine.

### Making Requests
> 〜をお願いできますか？
> (~ wo onegai dekimasu ka?)
> Would you be able to ~?

> 〜していただけませんか？
> (~ shite itadakemasen ka?)
> Would you please ~? (polite-leaning toward keigo)

> 〜てください。
> (~ te kudasai.)
> Please ~. (standard polite request)

### Offering and Receiving

> どうぞ。
> (Dōzo.)
> Please, go ahead. / Here you are.

> ありがとうございます。
> (Arigatō gozaimasu.)
> Thank you very much.

> いただきます。/ ごちそうさまでした。
> (Itadakimasu. / Gochisōsama deshita.)
> Thank you for the meal (before / after eating).

### Apologies
> 申し訳ありません。
> (Mōshiwake arimasen.)
> I'm very sorry. (Stronger than すみません)

> ご迷惑をおかけしました。
> (Gomeiwaku wo okake shimashita.)
> I'm sorry for the trouble I've caused.

> 失礼いたしました。
> (Shitsurei itashimashita.)
> Excuse me for my rudeness. (Formal apology for a minor breach)

## Polite Vocabulary Swaps

Some words have a polite counterpart that you use when speaking *politely about or to others*:

| Casual/neutral | Polite version | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 家 (ie/uchi) | お宅 (otaku) | house / home |
| 名前 (namae) | お名前 (onamae) | name |
| 弁当 (bentō) | お弁当 (obentō) | lunchbox |
| 金 (kane) | お金 (okane) | money |
| 茶 (cha) | お茶 (ocha) | tea |
| 腹 (hara) | お腹 (onaka) | stomach |
| 酒 (sake) | お酒 (osake) | alcohol |
| 見舞い (mimai) | お見舞い (omimai) | visiting the sick |
| 祝い (iwai) | お祝い (oiwai) | celebration/gift |
| 礼 (rei) | お礼 (orei) | thanks/gratitude |

The お (o-) or ご (go-) prefix adds a layer of politeness. It's used:
- With words relating to the other person
- With words relating to shared experiences (meals, events)
- NOT typically with foreign loanwords (おコーヒー sounds odd; コーヒー is fine)

## Asking Politely vs. Casually

The same request in three styles:

**"Could you show me this?"**

| Level | Japanese | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Casual | 見せて | Just the te-form — direct |
| Polite | 見せてください | Standard polite |
| More polite | 見せていただけますか？ | Bordered on keigo |
| Keigo | お見せいただけますでしょうか？ | Full keigo |

## Polite Telephone Japanese

Phone calls in Japanese have specific conventions:

**Answering the phone (private):**
> はい、田中です。
> (Hai, Tanaka desu.)
> Yes, this is Tanaka.

**Answering the phone (business):**
> お電話ありがとうございます。〇〇会社でございます。
> (Odenwa arigatō gozaimasu. 〇〇 kaisha de gozaimasu.)
> Thank you for calling. This is 〇〇 Company.

**Asking for someone:**
> 〇〇さんはいらっしゃいますか？
> (〇〇-san wa irasshaimasu ka?)
> Is 〇〇 there?

**Saying you'll call back:**
> 後ほどまたご連絡いたします。
> (Nochihodo mata go-renraku itashimasu.)
> I'll contact you again later.

## Polite Written Japanese (Emails & Letters)

Written polite Japanese has set phrases for openings and closings:

### Email Opening Phrases
| Phrase | Use |
|---|---|
| お世話になっております。 | "Thank you for your continued support" — standard business opener |
| いつもお世話になっております。 | "Always thank you for your support" — the most common opener |
| 突然のご連絡失礼いたします。 | "I apologize for this sudden contact" — cold outreach |
| ご無沙汰しております。 | "It's been a while" — reconnecting with someone |

### Email Closing Phrases
| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| よろしくお願いいたします。 | Please take care of this / Best regards |
| ご確認よろしくお願いいたします。 | Please review / Kindly check |
| 引き続きよろしくお願いいたします。 | Continued regards |
| お返事お待ちしております。 | I look forward to your reply |

### Letter Opening Phrases (very formal)
> 拝啓　時下ますますご清祥のことと存じます。
> (Haikei. Jika masumasu go-seishō no koto to zonjimasu.)
> Dear [recipient], I trust this letter finds you in good health.

This level of formality is used in official letters, wedding invitations, and legal correspondence. In everyday email, start with お世話になっております and you'll be fine.

## Polite Refusals

Japanese polite refusals almost never involve saying a direct "no." Instead:

| Refusal style | Example | Translation |
|---|---|---|
| Trailing negative | ちょっと難しくて… | It's a bit difficult... |
| Expressing difficulty | なかなか難しい状況でして… | The situation makes it rather difficult... |
| Expressing consideration | 検討してみます。 | I'll think about it. (Often means no) |
| Redirecting | また次の機会に。 | Perhaps another time. |
| Expressing regret | あいにく〜でして… | Unfortunately, ~ |

The silence after a trailing off is an answer. Don't press for clarification — that's considered rude.

## Polite Japanese in Service Contexts

Japan's service industry uses an ultra-polite sub-dialect called **丁寧語 (teinego)** at its most elevated — a few degrees below full keigo:

> いらっしゃいませ！
> (Irasshaimase!)
> Welcome! (Staff greeting — you don't need to respond)

> こちらへどうぞ。
> (Kochira e dōzo.)
> This way, please.

> ただいまご案内いたします。
> (Tadaima go-annai itashimasu.)
> I'll guide you right away.

> 少々お待ちくださいませ。
> (Shōshō omachi kudasaimase.)
> Please wait just a moment.

> ご注文はお決まりでしょうか？
> (Go-chūmon wa okimari deshō ka?)
> Have you decided on your order?

> ありがとうございました。またのお越しをお待ちしております。
> (Arigatō gozaimashita. Mata no okoshi wo omachi shite orimasu.)
> Thank you very much. We look forward to your next visit.

## Moving Between Polite and Casual

The shift from polite to casual usually happens gradually as a relationship develops. Signs that someone is inviting a shift:
- They start dropping です/ます themselves
- They ask "タメ語でいいよ" (You can talk casually)
- They're clearly your age or younger and relaxed
- The conversation has moved to very personal topics

When in doubt: stay polite. You can never offend someone by being too polite.
