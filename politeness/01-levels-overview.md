# Politeness Levels — The System That Runs Everything

> *"In English you might speak differently to your boss vs. your friends. In Japanese, those differences are baked into the grammar itself. Get the level wrong and you're either rude or robotic."*

Politeness in Japanese isn't just about choosing nice words — it's a grammatical system. Verb forms change. Vocabulary changes. Even which word you use for "I" or "you" signals your social register. Understanding this system is essential to sounding natural in any Japanese environment.

---

## Why This System Exists

Japanese society has historically organized itself around relationships: insider/outsider (内/外 — uchi/soto), senior/junior (先輩/後輩 — senpai/kōhai), and social hierarchy. The language evolved to encode these relationships directly.

Crucially, **the appropriate level depends on the relationship, not just the situation.** You might use casual Japanese in a formal place (talking to a close friend at a fancy restaurant) or polite Japanese in an informal place (talking to an unfamiliar person at a festival).

The key factors:
1. **Familiarity** — How well do you know this person?
2. **Social hierarchy** — Are they senior/junior to you?
3. **Group membership** — Are they inside your group (uchi) or outside (soto)?
4. **Context** — Is this a professional, social, or personal situation?

---

## The Three Major Levels

### Level 1: Casual / Informal (タメ語 — tamego)
**Used with:** Close friends, family members, people younger than you in casual settings, pets

- Verbs in plain form (行く、食べる、来る)
- Copula: だ instead of です
- Particles often dropped
- Contractions common (〜てる、〜ちゃう)
- First-person pronoun: 俺 (ore) for males, うち (uchi) / あたし (atashi) for females, or just drop it entirely

### Level 2: Polite / Standard (丁寧語 — teinego)
**Used with:** Strangers, acquaintances, people you've just met, in service contexts, mild professional settings

- Verbs in masu-form (行きます、食べます、来ます)
- Copula: です
- Particles mostly present
- First-person: 私 (watashi)

This is the level taught in most Japanese textbooks. It's the safe default for any new relationship.

### Level 3: Honorific / Formal (敬語 — keigo)
**Used with:** Superiors, clients, important guests, formal occasions, elderly people, people you respect highly

- Special vocabulary (行く → いらっしゃる、言う → おっしゃる、食べる → 召し上がる)
- Complex auxiliary constructions (〜でいらっしゃる、〜ていただく)
- Much longer sentences
- First-person: 私 (watashi) or 弊社 (heisha — "our humble company") in business

Keigo is a system within itself — covered in detail in [Honorific Japanese →](04-honorific-japanese.md).

## The "I" Problem

Japanese has many first-person pronouns. The one you use says a lot:

| Pronoun | Who uses it | Impression |
|---|---|---|
| 私 (watashi) | Everyone, polite | Standard, neutral |
| 僕 (boku) | Males, soft/gentle | Boyish, somewhat humble |
| 俺 (ore) | Males, casual | Masculine, direct, casual |
| 俺様 (ore-sama) | Arrogant characters in manga | "I, the magnificent" — never sincere |
| うち (uchi) | Females (esp. Kansai) | Casual, regional |
| あたし (atashi) | Females, casual | Soft, feminine |
| あたくし (atakushi) | Very formal, older females | Ultra-formal, elegant |
| 自分 (jibun) | Military / masculine / Osaka | "Myself" — contextual |
| わし (washi) | Older males / dialect | Aged, regional feel |
| 我が (waga) | Written/formal | "Our ~" — 我が国 = "our nation" |

## The "You" Problem (Avoid あなた)

This surprises many learners: **あなた (anata) is awkward in most real-world contexts.** Using it directly to address someone sounds either distant, confrontational, or like a soap opera.

What Japanese people actually say:

| Situation | What to use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Addressing someone | Use their name + さん | 田中さんは...? |
| Addressing a superior | Their title | 先生は... / 部長は... |
| To a close partner | あなた (affectionate, traditional) | まあ、あなた... |
| Writing/lyrics | あなた (poetic, fine in text) | あなたを愛してる |
| In a group | 皆さん (mina-san) | 皆さんはどうですか？ |

When in doubt: use their name. Always.

## The Uchi/Soto Dimension (内/外)

This is one of the most important cultural concepts behind politeness:

- **内 (uchi)** = inside / my group (family, close friends, your company)
- **外 (soto)** = outside / other groups (strangers, other companies, society)

The rule: **elevate outsiders, humble your insiders.**

This means when talking to someone from another company about your own colleague:
> 「田中は只今席を外しております。」
> "Tanaka is currently away from his desk."
> (No さん for Tanaka — he's uchi, so you humble him)

But when talking about the same colleague to him directly:
> 「田中さん、お客様がいらっしゃいました。」
> "Tanaka-san, a customer has arrived."
> (さん returns because now you're speaking TO him, not ABOUT him to an outsider)

This dimension is what trips up learners most: the same person gets different treatment depending on who's listening.

## Social Situations and Their Default Levels

| Situation | Default level | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Meeting someone for the first time | Polite (丁寧語) | Always start polite, let the other person signal a shift to casual |
| At a convenience store | Polite | Basic politeness expected |
| With close friends | Casual | Staying formal with close friends is awkward and distancing |
| With your boss | Polite → Keigo | Depends on company culture |
| With a client | Keigo | Non-negotiable in business |
| In a job interview | Keigo | Use it throughout |
| Sending a work email | Polite → Keigo | Formal written style |
| On social media | Casual | Personal accounts are casual; business accounts are polite |
| Talking to elderly people you don't know | Polite | Respectful default |
| In a group with mixed familiarity | Polite | Accommodate the least familiar person |

## When Levels Get Mixed Up: The Consequences

Getting the level wrong has real social consequences:

**Too casual too soon:** Rude, presumptuous, especially with seniors or in professional settings. Creates immediate awkwardness.

**Too formal with close friends:** Creates distance, feels cold or sarcastic. Like responding to "you coming out tonight?" with "I am unable to attend this evening."

**Using keigo incorrectly:** Can actually be worse than not using it — garbled keigo sounds condescending or bizarre. If you're not sure, polite (丁寧語) is always acceptable.

**The good news:** Japanese people are generally very forgiving of foreign speakers' level mistakes. A sincere attempt matters more than perfection.

---

## Code-Switching: How Japanese People Do It

Native speakers shift levels seamlessly mid-conversation. They might:

- Use casual Japanese chatting with a coworker at lunch
- Switch to polite as soon as a manager walks up
- Use full keigo when the client calls
- Switch back to casual in a message to the same coworker five minutes later

This code-switching is natural, constant, and doesn't feel like effort to natives. For learners, the goal isn't to always be in the right register — it's to gradually develop sensitivity to which register the situation calls for.
