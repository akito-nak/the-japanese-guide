# Emergencies — Phrases You Hope You Never Need

> *"Japan is remarkably safe. Crime rates are low, hospitals are excellent, and public services work. But emergencies happen everywhere, and knowing what to say — or recognizing what's being said — can make a critical difference."*

Bookmark this page. Print it. Save it to your phone offline. You won't need it 99% of the time, and you absolutely want it for that 1%.

---

## Emergency Numbers

| Number | Service | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **110** | Police (警察) | Crime, accidents, suspicious activity |
| **119** | Fire & Ambulance (救急) | Both fire and medical emergencies |
| **118** | Japan Coast Guard | Maritime emergencies |
| **#9110** | Police consultation | Non-emergency police line |

**Important:** English support is available at these numbers, but response varies by region. Some operators will immediately connect you to an interpreter. Speak clearly and slowly.

**Tourist emergency hotline:**
- **Japan Tourism Agency:** 050-3816-2787 (24 hours, multiple languages)
- **JNTO:** Japan National Tourism Organization operates regional multilingual lines

## Medical Emergencies (病院・救急)

### Calling an Ambulance
> 救急車を呼んでください！
> (Kyūkyūsha wo yonde kudasai!)
> **Please call an ambulance!**

> 〜に怪我をしました。
> (~ni kega wo shimashita.)
> I have an injury to my ~.

> 意識がありません。
> (Ishiki ga arimasen.)
> They are unconscious.

> 胸が痛いです。
> (Mune ga itai desu.)
> Chest pain.

> 息ができません。
> (Iki ga dekimasen.)
> I can't breathe.

> 〜にアレルギーがあります。
> (~ni arerugī ga arimasu.)
> I'm allergic to ~.

> 薬は〜を飲んでいます。
> (Kusuri wa ~ wo nonde imasu.)
> I'm taking ~ medication.

### Key Medical Phrases
> 保険はありますか？ — Do you have insurance?
> 旅行保険があります。 — I have travel insurance.
> ここが痛いです。 — It hurts here. [point]
> 熱があります。 — I have a fever.
> 吐き気がします。 — I feel nauseous.
> 下痢をしています。 — I have diarrhea.
> 妊娠しています。 — I am pregnant.
> 血液型はAです。 — My blood type is A.

**Travel insurance tip:** Japan's healthcare is excellent but not free for non-residents. Even a routine ER visit can cost ¥5,000–¥30,000 or more. Comprehensive travel insurance is strongly recommended.

## Crime and Police (警察)

Japan is one of the world's safest countries. Violent crime involving tourists is rare. Common issues: pickpocketing (rare but exists in crowded areas), lost items, and occasional scams in entertainment districts.

### Calling Police / Reporting a Crime
> 助けてください！
> (Tasukete kudasai!)
> **Help me!**

> 泥棒です！
> (Dorobō desu!)
> Thief! (泥棒 = thief)

> 警察を呼んでください！
> (Keisatsu wo yonde kudasai!)
> **Please call the police!**

> 財布をすられました。
> (Saifu wo suraremashita.)
> My wallet was stolen. (Pickpocketed)

> パスポートをなくしました。
> (Pasupōto wo nakushimashita.)
> I lost my passport.

> 〜に痴漢されました。
> (~ni chikan saremashita.)
> I was groped/molested by ~. (Chikan is a serious crime in Japan)

### At a Police Box (交番 — Kōban)
Japan has an extensive network of 交番 (kōban) — small neighborhood police boxes staffed by local officers. These are your first port of call for:
- Lost items (日本人は届け出る — Japanese people return lost items at extraordinary rates)
- Directions
- Non-emergency reports

> 忘れ物をしました。 — I left something behind.
> 財布が見つかりましたか？ — Has my wallet been found?
> 盗難届を出したいです。 — I'd like to file a theft report.

**Lost and Found:** Japan's lost-and-found system is remarkable. If you lose something on a train, call the terminal station. Wallets with cash are routinely turned in intact.

## Natural Disasters — 自然災害 (Shizen Saigai)

Japan experiences earthquakes, typhoons, tsunami, and volcanic activity. This isn't rare — it's part of daily life for Japanese people, and the emergency systems are excellent.

### Earthquakes (地震 — Jishin)

**If indoors during a major earthquake:**
1. Get under a sturdy table or desk (身を守る — mi wo mamoru)
2. Stay away from windows
3. Do NOT run outside during shaking
4. If the shaking stops, exit carefully — check for structural damage

**If outdoors:**
1. Move away from buildings, utility poles, trees
2. Protect your head
3. Move to open ground

**Earthquake alerts:**
Japan's 緊急地震速報 (Kinkyu Jishin Sokuho — Earthquake Early Warning) system sends alerts to all mobile phones seconds before shaking begins. You'll hear a distinctive alarming tone. This system genuinely saves lives.

**Earthquake vocabulary:**
| Japanese | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 地震 (jishin) | Earthquake |
| 震度 (shindo) | Seismic intensity (Japanese scale 1–7, not Richter) |
| マグニチュード | Magnitude |
| 震源地 (shingenchi) | Epicenter |
| 津波 (tsunami) | Tsunami |
| 避難 (hinan) | Evacuation |
| 避難場所 (hinanbasho) | Evacuation site |
| 余震 (yoshin) | Aftershock |

**Tsunami warning:**
> 津波注意報 (tsunami chūihō) — Tsunami watch
> 津波警報 (tsunami keihō) — Tsunami warning
> 大津波警報 (ōtsunami keihō) — Major tsunami warning

**If you hear a tsunami warning:** Get to high ground immediately. Don't wait to see the water. Tsunamis move faster than people expect and the first wave is rarely the largest.

### Typhoons (台風 — Taifū)

Typhoon season: July–October (peaks September). Japan is directly in the Pacific typhoon belt.

**When a typhoon is coming:**
- Trains and flights will be cancelled for major typhoons
- Convenience stores will run out of emergency supplies (bread, batteries, water)
- 暴風域 (bōfūiki) — storm zone — avoid being outdoors
- Plan to stay indoors

**Typhoon vocabulary:**
| Japanese | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 暴風雨 (bōfūu) | Violent storm |
| 大雨 (ōame) | Heavy rain |
| 洪水 (kōzui) | Flood |
| 土砂崩れ (dosha kuzure) | Landslide |
| 避難勧告 (hinan kankoku) | Evacuation advisory |
| 避難指示 (hinan shiji) | Evacuation order (mandatory) |

### Emergency Alerts on Your Phone
Enable **J-Alert** (全国瞬時警報システム) if possible — Japan's missile and natural disaster alert system. All phones in Japan receive alerts automatically, but you may need to enable location services for your phone number in Japan.

> Fire!

> (Nigete kudasai!)
> Run! / Evacuate!

> (Hito ga naka ni imasu.)
> There is someone inside.

**Fire emergency number: 119**

## Lost or Missing Person (迷子 — Maigo)

If you lose a child or someone loses you:

> 子供がいなくなりました。
> (Kodomo ga inaku narimashita.)
> My child is missing.

> 迷子になりました。
> (Maigo ni narimashita.)
> I'm lost.

At train stations and department stores, there are **迷子センター (maigo sentā)** — lost children's centers. Announcements over the PA system for lost children use this phrase:

> 迷子のお知らせをいたします。〇〇ちゃん、〇歳のお子様をお預かりしています。
> "We have an announcement for a lost child. We have [child's name], a [age]-year-old child in our care."

## Practical Preparation

Before visiting Japan, do these:

1. **Save emergency numbers** in your phone (110, 119)
2. **Photo your passport** and store it in the cloud
3. **Get travel insurance** with medical evacuation coverage
4. **Note your hotel address in Japanese** — taxi drivers may not read English
5. **Download Google Translate with Japanese offline** — camera translation mode reads signs
6. **Check travel advisories** for typhoon season and earthquake-prone areas

Japan is safe. But prepared is better.
