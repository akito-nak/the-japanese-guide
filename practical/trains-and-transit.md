# Trains & Transit — Getting Around Japan

> *"Japan's train system is the greatest in the world. It's also complex enough to intimidate first-timers. But once you understand the logic, it's intuitive — and you'll miss it when you leave."*

Japan moves by train. The network is vast, punctual, clean, and covers virtually every corner of the country. Understanding how it works is essential for visiting, and it's loaded with useful vocabulary.

---

## The Basics: Types of Trains

Japan's rail network is operated by dozens of companies. The major categories:

| Type | Japanese | Description |
|---|---|---|
| JR (Japan Railways) | JR | National network — runs the Shinkansen and connects major cities |
| 私鉄 (shitetsu) | Private Railways | Company-owned lines — Tokyo has ~10 major ones |
| 地下鉄 (chikatetsu) | Subway/Metro | Underground lines — Tokyo Metro, Osaka Metro |
| 新幹線 (shinkansen) | Bullet Train | High-speed intercity trains |
| モノレール (monorēru) | Monorail | Airport connections, urban routes |
| バス (basu) | Bus | Supplements trains, essential in rural areas |

## IC Cards — The Magic Card

**IC cards** (ICカード) are prepaid contactless transit cards. The major ones:

| Card | Where | Works |
|---|---|---|
| Suica (スイカ) | Tokyo / JR East | Nationwide |
| PASMO (パスモ) | Tokyo private railways | Nationwide |
| ICOCA (イコカ) | Osaka/Kansai | Nationwide |
| Manaca (マナカ) | Nagoya | Nationwide |
| TOICA (トイカ) | Nagoya/JR Central | Nationwide |

**Important:** Since 2023, you can add Suica to Apple Wallet and Google Pay on your phone. This is the easiest option for visitors.

**Using an IC card:**
- Touch the sensor at the entry gate → green light → pass through
- Touch again at the exit gate → fare is automatically deducted
- You can also use IC cards to pay at konbini, vending machines, and some shops
- When card is low: 乗り越し清算機 (norioeshi seisanki — fare adjustment machine) at exit

## Reading a Train Map

Japanese train maps use kanji station names — learning the kanji for your destination is essential.

**Pro tip:** Every station has a **station number** (letters + numbers, e.g., JY01, G03). This is displayed alongside the kanji name. Look for your number if the kanji is unfamiliar.

**The station structure:**
```
路線名 (Line Name) ← You need to know which line
駅名 (Station Name) ← Your destination  
番線 (Platform Number) ← Which platform
```

### Tokyo's Key Lines
| Line | Notable stations | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 山手線 (Yamanote) | Tokyo, Shinjuku, Shibuya, Harajuku, Akihabara | The green loop — connects all major hubs |
| 中央線 (Chūō) | Tokyo → Shinjuku → Kichijoji | Red/orange line heading west |
| 東海道線 (Tōkaidō) | Tokyo → Yokohama | South/southwest |
| 総武線 (Sōbu) | Tokyo → Akihabara → Ochanomizu | Yellow line, east-west |
| 丸ノ内線 (Marunouchi) | Shinjuku → Tokyo (metro) | Red Tokyo Metro line |
| 渋谷から各線 | Shibuya hub | Multiple private lines from Shibuya |

## Platform Announcements — What You'll Hear

Japanese station announcements follow a pattern. Here's what to listen for:

### On the Platform
> 〇〇番線に〇〇行き〇〇が参ります。黄色い線の内側にお下がりください。
> "The [bound for destination] [train type] will arrive on track [number]. Please step back behind the yellow line."

> ドアが閉まります。ご注意ください。
> "Doors are closing. Please be careful."

> まもなく〇〇番線に列車が参ります。
> "A train will arrive at platform [number] shortly."

### On the Train
> 次は〇〇、〇〇です。
> "Next is [station name], [station name]."
> (Repeated in Japanese then often English on major lines)

> お乗り換えの方は...
> "For passengers transferring to..."

> この電車は〇〇行きです。
> "This train is bound for [destination]."

> まもなく〇〇に到着します。
> "We will soon arrive at [station]."

> 終点、〇〇です。全員降りてください。
> "This is the final stop, [station]. All passengers please exit."

## The Shinkansen (新幹線)

The bullet train is one of Japan's great achievements — and a great experience:

| Line | Route | Max speed |
|---|---|---|
| 東海道新幹線 (Tōkaidō) | Tokyo → Osaka (via Nagoya, Kyoto) | 285 km/h |
| 山陽新幹線 (Sanyō) | Osaka → Hiroshima → Fukuoka | 300 km/h |
| 東北新幹線 (Tōhoku) | Tokyo → Sendai → Morioka → Aomori | 320 km/h |
| 北陸新幹線 (Hokuriku) | Tokyo → Kanazawa → Fukui (new) | 260 km/h |
| 九州新幹線 (Kyūshū) | Fukuoka → Kagoshima | 260 km/h |
| 北海道新幹線 (Hokkaidō) | Aomori → Hakodate → Sapporo | 260 km/h |

### Shinkansen Types (Tōkaidō Line, Tokyo–Osaka)
| Name | Stops |
|---|---|
| のぞみ (Nozomi) | Fewest — fastest (Tokyo → Osaka in ~2h20m) |
| ひかり (Hikari) | More stops |
| こだま (Kodama) | All stops — slowest |

**JR Pass:** If you're visiting Japan, a JR Pass (purchased outside Japan) can cover unlimited Shinkansen travel. Do the math — for multiple intercity trips it pays for itself.

**Buying tickets:**
- 自動券売機 (jidō kenbaiki) — vending machine
- みどりの窓口 (midori no madoguchi) — "green counter" — staffed JR ticket office
- Online at smart-ex.jp (for the Tōkaidō/Sanyō lines)

## Trains at Night: The Last Train (終電)

In Japan, **missing the last train** (終電を逃す — shūden wo nogasu) is a real concern. Trains stop running at midnight or earlier in most cities. The concept is important enough that:

- Izakaya TV ads always show people drinking and then rushing for the last train
- Many people plan their evening around the last train time
- Taxis after midnight are expensive (and fare can increase ~20%)
- Some people take **漫画喫茶 (manga kissa)** — internet cafes — as emergency overnight stays

Check last train times on Google Maps Japan or Hyperdia.

## Etiquette on Trains

Japanese train etiquette is quietly strict:

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Line up on the platform markers | Eat on commuter trains (long-distance shinkansen is fine) |
| Let passengers exit before boarding | Talk loudly on the phone (step off at a station if you must) |
| Give up priority seats (優先席) to those who need them | Put your bag on a seat during rush hour |
| Set phone to manner mode (マナーモード) | Play audio without headphones |
| Be quiet and still | Push when boarding/exiting |

**Women-only cars (女性専用車両):** Many lines have women-only cars during peak hours. Marked prominently. Men who enter are politely but firmly redirected.

**Crowded trains during rush hour:** Tokyo rush hour (7:30–9:00am on major lines) is genuinely intense. Train staff (押し屋 — oshiya, "pushers") sometimes help compress passengers into cars. It's uncomfortable and everyone knows it.

## Buses (バス)

Buses are essential in smaller cities and rural areas. The system varies by region:

**Paying:**
- **Flat fare (均一料金 — kin'itsu ryōkin):** Drop payment in the box when you board
- **Zone fare:** Take a numbered ticket when boarding, pay based on number when exiting

> 整理券をお取りください。
> "Please take a numbered ticket."

> 〇番の方はいくらですか？
> "How much is ticket number ~?"

**Hailing a bus:**
In some rural areas you must wave (手を挙げる — te wo ageru) to stop the bus. Don't stand stiffly hoping it will stop — it won't.

## Taxis (タクシー)

Japanese taxis are clean, reliable, and expensive:
- **Red/orange flag light** (空車 — kūsha) = available
- **Green light** (賃走 — chinso) = occupied
- **Door opens automatically** — do NOT try to open or close it yourself

> 〜までお願いします。 — To ~, please.
> ここで降ります。 — I'll get off here.
> 領収書をください。 — Receipt please.
> メーターで行ってください。 — By meter, please.

**Important:** Car-sharing services like Uber exist in Japan but are restricted. In most areas, taxis are the only option.
