# Shopping & Money — Life in Japan's Consumer Paradise

> *"Japan is simultaneously one of the best and most expensive places to shop in the world. It depends entirely on what you're shopping for and where."*

Shopping in Japan is an experience in itself — whether you're in a hyaku-en (100-yen) shop buying everything imaginable for ¥110, navigating a seven-floor department store, or finding vintage treasures in a back-alley shop. Here's everything you need.

---

## Money Basics — お金 (Okane)

Japan remains largely **cash-based** compared to most developed countries, though this is changing fast.

### Currency
| Coin/Note | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1円 (ichi-en) | ¥1 | Lightweight aluminum |
| 5円 (go-en) | ¥5 | Hole in center, used as good luck |
| 10円 (jū-en) | ¥10 | Bronze |
| 50円 (go-jū-en) | ¥50 | Hole in center |
| 100円 (hyaku-en) | ¥100 | Common silver coin |
| 500円 (go-hyaku-en) | ¥500 | Most valuable coin, updated design |
| 1,000円 (sen-en) | ¥1,000 | Note — Noguchi Hideyo |
| 5,000円 (go-sen-en) | ¥5,000 | Note — Higuchi Ichiyo |
| 10,000円 (ichi-man-en) | ¥10,000 | Highest denomination — Fukuzawa Yukichi |

**Japan uses 万 (man = 10,000) as its base unit.** A ¥30,000 item is 三万円 (sanman-en), not "thirty thousand yen." This is important when reading prices.

### ATMs
- **International cards:** Works at **コンビニATM** (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson) and **Japan Post ATMs (ゆうちょATM)**
- Most other ATMs (especially bank ATMs) don't accept foreign cards
- 7-Bank ATMs (inside 7-Eleven) are the most reliable internationally

### Cashless Payment
Japan is adopting cashless rapidly:
| Method | Brand | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| IC Cards | Suica, PASMO | Also accepted at many shops |
| QR Code | PayPay, LINE Pay, Rakuten Pay | Huge in Japan since 2018 |
| Credit Card | Visa, Mastercard | Widely accepted in cities now |
| NFC/Tap | Apple Pay, Google Pay | Works at most konbini and convenience |

## Types of Stores

### コンビニ (Konbini) — Convenience Stores
The backbone of daily life in Japan. Open 24/7, they sell almost everything:

**The major chains:**
| Chain | Notes |
|---|---|
| セブンイレブン (7-Eleven) | Considered best food quality, most ATMs |
| ファミリーマート (FamilyMart) | Strong on hot foods, brand collabs |
| ローソン (Lawson) | Strong selection, Lawson 100 (¥100 grocery section) |

**What konbini sells:**
- Hot foods: nikuman (meat buns), oden, karaage, fries
- Onigiri (rice balls), sandwiches, sushi
- Ready-to-eat bento
- All beverages including alcohol
- Stationery, medicine, cosmetics
- ATMs, fax, printing, paying bills
- Buying event/concert tickets
- Sending packages (宅急便 — takkyūbin)

### スーパーマーケット (Sūpā) — Supermarkets
For regular groceries. Major chains: **イオン (Aeon)**, **ライフ (Life)**, **西友 (Seiyu)**, **OK ストア (OKStore)**. Discount hours: fresh items go on sale (割引 — waribiki) in the evening before closing.

### デパート (Depāto) — Department Stores
Multiple floors of everything, with immaculate service and corresponding prices. The **地下 (chika = basement)** is the most important floor — the depachika (デパ地下) is a food paradise of sweets, prepared foods, and takeaway dishes. Worth visiting even if you don't buy.

Major chains: **伊勢丹 (Isetan)**, **高島屋 (Takashimaya)**, **三越 (Mitsukoshi)**, **大丸 (Daimaru)**

### 100円ショップ (Hyaku-en Shoppu) — 100-Yen Shops
Everything ¥100–¥330. Quality varies but often excellent for kitchen goods, stationery, seasonal items, travel accessories.

Major chains: **ダイソー (Daiso)**, **セリア (Seria)**, **キャンドゥ (Can Do)**

Daiso has become internationally popular. Their Japanese packaging makes excellent listening practice.

### ドンキホーテ (Donki / Don Quijote)
The chaotic discount emporium. Open until midnight or 24 hours. Stacked floor-to-ceiling with everything imaginable — electronics, cosmetics, food, costumes, luggage, tax-free goods for tourists. The theme song will live in your head forever:
> ♪ ドン・ドン・ドン・ドンキ！ドン・キホーテ！

### 古着屋 (Furugiya) — Vintage/Secondhand Shops
Japan's vintage clothing culture is world-class. **下北沢 (Shimokitazawa)** in Tokyo and **アメリカ村 (Amerika-Mura)** in Osaka are the major vintage districts.

**BOOKOFF (ブックオフ):** Japan's secondhand bookstore chain — also sells CDs, games, manga, and increasingly clothing. Excellent and cheap.

## Tax-Free Shopping (免税 — Menzei)

Non-Japanese residents can receive consumption tax (消費税 — shōhizei, currently 10%) refunds on purchases over ¥5,000 at participating stores:

1. Show your **passport** at the register
2. Ask **「免税でお願いします」(menzei de onegaishimasu)**
3. Items may be sealed in a bag until you leave Japan
4. Keep receipts — may be checked at the airport

Large stores, electronics retailers (ヨドバシカメラ、ビックカメラ — Yodobashi Camera, Bic Camera), and pharmacies commonly offer this.

## Electronics Shopping

**Akihabara (秋葉原)** in Tokyo is the world capital of consumer electronics, though Osaka's **日本橋 (Nippombashi)** and Nagoya's **大須 (Ōsu)** are strong competitors.

Major electronics chains:
| Chain | Notes |
|---|---|
| ヨドバシカメラ | Large, good prices, wide selection |
| ビックカメラ | Similar to Yodobashi |
| ノジマ | Regional chain, competitive |
| エディオン | Strong in Kansai |
| ヤマダ電機 | Japan's largest electronics chain |

**Tip:** For cameras, laptops, and audio gear, prices in Japan (especially at gray market/used) are often significantly lower than Western markets.

## Pharmacy and Drugstores

Japanese drugstores (ドラッグストア — doraggu sutoa) sell cosmetics, OTC medicine, snacks, and a surprisingly good selection of food:

| Chain | Notes |
|---|---|
| マツモトキヨシ (Matsumoto Kiyoshi) | Nationwide, strong cosmetics |
| ウエルシア (Welcia) | Large, food section |
| ツルハ (Tsuruha) | Strong in eastern Japan |
| コスモス (Cosmos) | Discount focus |

**Popular items to buy at Japanese pharmacies:**
- 龍角散 (Ryūkakusan) — throat drops / powder
- バンテリン (Vantelin) — muscle/joint pain
- 正露丸 (Seirogan) — stomach pills (extreme smell)
- SPF50+ sunscreen — Japan makes excellent formulas
- Hand cream — Japanese skincare generally excellent

## Bargaining Culture

**Don't bargain in Japanese shops.** The listed price is the price. Asking for a discount in a regular store will cause visible discomfort. Exceptions:
- **Flea markets (フリマ — furima)** and antique fairs — mild negotiation acceptable
- **Electronics for large purchases** — sometimes a minor discount if you ask politely
- **Outlet malls (アウトレット)** — price is already discounted

## Seasonal Sales

Japan has predictable sale seasons:
| Season | Period | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 冬のセール | January | Post-New Year sales |
| 福袋 (fukubukuro) | January 1–2 | "Lucky bags" — sealed bags of goods at discount |
| バレンタイン | February | Reverse in Japan: women give chocolate to men |
| ゴールデンウィーク | Late April–May | Crowded but some sales |
| 夏のセール | July | Summer clearance |
| ブラックフライデー | November | Growing in Japan since ~2016 |

**福袋 (fukubukuro)** — lucky bags — are a January tradition. Shops sell sealed bags containing multiple items (usually above their combined retail value). People queue from the previous night for popular brands.
