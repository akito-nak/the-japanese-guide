# Language Evolution — From Classical Japanese to Emoji

> *"Modern Japanese is the result of 1,500 years of borrowing, adapting, reforming, and inventing. Understanding how it changed explains why it is the way it is."*

Every quirk of Japanese — the three writing systems, the thousands of loanwords, the formal vs. casual divide — has a historical reason. Here's the story.

---

## 古典日本語 — Classical Japanese (上代・中古語)

The oldest forms of Japanese are preserved in texts from the 7th–8th centuries:

- **『万葉集』(Man'yōshū, 759 CE):** Japan's oldest poetry anthology, using kanji purely phonetically to write Japanese sounds — a system called **万葉仮名 (man'yōgana)**. This was the precursor to kana.
- **『古事記』(Kojiki, 712 CE):** Japan's oldest chronicle, mixing man'yōgana with classical Chinese.
- **『日本書紀』(Nihon Shoki, 720 CE):** More formal history, written predominantly in classical Chinese.

Classical Japanese (古典語 — kotenggo or 文語 — bungo) has verb forms and vocabulary that look almost nothing like modern Japanese:

| Classical | Modern | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| あり (ari) | ある (aru) | to exist / be |
| けり (keri) | だった (datta) | past tense marker |
| なり (nari) | だ / です (da / desu) | copula "is" |
| べし (beshi) | べきだ (beki da) | should / must |
| ず (zu) | ない (nai) | negative |
| ぬ (nu) | た (ta) | perfect aspect |

You'll still encounter classical forms in:
- Poetry (especially haiku and waka)
- Formal speeches and ceremonies
- Some set phrases: 何卒よろしく、ご健勝をお祈りいたします
- Literature studies in Japanese high school

## The Kana Revolution — 9th Century

Before kana, writing Japanese was deeply awkward: Chinese characters were used phonetically (man'yōgana) for Japanese sounds, requiring enormous effort per syllable.

In the early Heian period (794–1185 CE), two phonetic scripts emerged:

### Hiragana
Developed primarily by court women as cursive abbreviations of man'yōgana kanji:
- あ from 安 (an)
- い from 以 (i)
- う from 宇 (u)
- え from 衣 (e)
- お from 於 (o)

Because hiragana was used mainly by women writing in Japanese (men wrote formal texts in Chinese), it was initially called **女手 (onnade — "woman's hand")**.

The result: **The Tale of Genji (源氏物語)** — the world's first novel — was written in hiragana by a woman in the 11th century. Without the kana revolution, this would have been impossible.

### Katakana
Developed by male Buddhist monks as shorthand annotations on Chinese texts — a way to mark reading aids alongside kanji. Where hiragana simplified whole kanji characters, katakana extracted partial elements.

---

## The Kanji-Kana Hybrid System

Japanese settled into its distinctive mixed writing system by the 11th century:
- **Kanji** for meaning-carrying content words
- **Hiragana** for grammatical elements (particles, verb endings)
- **Katakana** for emphasis, foreign words (later)

This system has never been replaced — because it works, and because no reform was ever powerful enough to overcome cultural inertia.

## Chinese Borrowings — On-Yomi Waves

Japanese didn't borrow from Chinese all at once. Different waves of borrowing produced different pronunciation layers:

| Reading type | Origin period | Region | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 呉音 (Go-on) | 5th–6th century | Southern China (Wu) | 明 (myō/mō), 行 (gyō) |
| 漢音 (Kan-on) | 7th–9th century | Northern China (Tang) | 明 (mei), 行 (kō) |
| 唐音 (Tō-on) | 12th–17th century | Song/Ming dynasty | 明 (min), 行 (hin) |

This is why many kanji have multiple on-yomi readings — they each come from different periods and different regions of China.

**Result:** The word 行 (go) has at least three on-yomi:
- ぎょう (gyō) — 行列 (parade)
- こう (kō) — 銀行 (bank)
- あん (an) — rare, Buddhist context

## The Meiji Language Revolution

The Meiji Period (1868–1912) was the most dramatic period of language change since kana was invented.

### The Problem: How Do You Modernize a Language?
Japan needed vocabulary for concepts that didn't exist in Japanese: democracy, philosophy, chemistry, electricity, capitalism, liberty, republic, science...

### Solution 1: Kanji Compounds (漢語 — kango)
Japanese intellectuals and translators created new compound words from kanji to represent Western concepts:

| Western concept | Japanese creation | Reading |
|---|---|---|
| Telephone | 電話 (electricity + speech) | denwa |
| Democracy | 民主主義 | minshu shugi |
| Science | 科学 | kagaku |
| Philosophy | 哲学 | tetsugaku |
| Economy | 経済 | keizai |
| Society | 社会 | shakai |
| Individual | 個人 | kojin |
| Liberty | 自由 | jiyū |
| Revolution | 革命 | kakumei |
| Culture | 文化 | bunka |

Many of these compound words were then exported *back* to China, where they're now standard Chinese vocabulary. Japanese invented the Chinese word for "democracy" (民主主義) and many others. The irony is significant.

### Solution 2: Katakana Loanwords (外来語 — gairaigo)
For words that couldn't be easily translated, especially technology and food, katakana borrowing took over:

| Word | From | Era |
|---|---|---|
| ビール (bīru) | German Bier | 1850s |
| パン (pan) | Portuguese pão | 1500s |
| コーヒー (kōhī) | Dutch koffie | 1700s |
| アルバイト (arubaito) | German Arbeit | Meiji |
| テレビ (terebi) | English television | 1950s |
| スマホ (sumaho) | English smartphone | 2010s |

### Language Standardization
The Meiji government standardized a national language (国語 — kokugo) based on educated Tokyo speech:
- Regional dialects suppressed in schools
- National literacy campaigns raised reading rates dramatically
- Newspapers and novels written in the new standard
- A new style called **言文一致 (genbun-itchi — "unifying written and spoken language")** emerged — writing became closer to how people actually spoke

Before Meiji, formal written Japanese and spoken Japanese were completely different languages. After genbun-itchi, they converged.

## Postwar Language Reform

After WWII, the US Occupation and the Japanese government reformed the writing system:

### 当用漢字 (Tōyō Kanji, 1946)
1850 kanji designated for general use. Simplified forms introduced for some characters. Schools were instructed to teach only these kanji.

### 現代かなづかい (Gendai Kanazukai, 1946)
Kana spelling was reformed to match modern pronunciation. Some historical spellings that no longer matched pronunciation were changed:
- Historical: ゐ (wi), ゑ (we) — these sounds no longer exist; replaced with い, え
- The particle は (wa as topic marker) kept its spelling even though it's pronounced "wa" not "ha"
- ヲ kept as particle を, even though modern pronunciation is "o"

### 常用漢字 (Jōyō Kanji, 1981, revised 2010)
The official list of kanji for general use — currently 2,136 characters. This is the benchmark for "literacy" in Japan.

## The Gairaigo Explosion — Postwar English

After 1945, American English flooded Japanese through occupation, media, and pop culture:

**1940s–50s (direct occupation terms):**
ジープ (jeep), バー (bar), ダンス (dance), デモ (demo/demonstration), ストライキ (strike)

**1960s–70s (mass media and consumerism):**
テレビ (TV), コンピューター (computer), マネージャー (manager), スーパー (supermarket)

**1980s–90s (economic boom):**
リストラ (restructuring — from "restructure"), バブル (bubble), パワーハラスメント → パワハラ (power harassment), セクシャルハラスメント → セクハラ (sexual harassment)

**2000s–2010s (internet age):**
スマホ (smartphone), ツイート (tweet), シェア (share), フォロー (follow), バズる (to go viral, from "buzz")

The rate of loanword adoption has accelerated with the internet. New English terms now enter Japanese within months.

## The Emoji Revolution (絵文字)

**絵文字 (emoji — "picture character")** was invented in Japan in 1999 by NTT DoCoMo engineer Shigetaka Kurita. The first set had 176 characters for mobile phones.

When Apple included emoji support in iOS 5 (2011), they went global — and fundamentally changed digital communication worldwide. The word itself is now in the Oxford English Dictionary.

The name comes from:
- 絵 (e) — picture
- 文字 (moji) — character / letter

## The Future of Japanese

Modern Japanese continues to evolve rapidly:

**Gender-neutral language:** Traditional gendered speech patterns (see [Spoken vs. Written](../foundations/07-spoken-vs-written.md)) are fading among younger speakers. Gender-neutral forms are increasingly common.

**English influence:** Some argue Japanese is being "Anglicized" — more katakana, less classical phrasing. Others see this as natural evolution.

**Population decline:** With fewer speakers, Japanese may face long-term pressure. But Japanese media, pop culture, and language learning have never been more global.

**AI and Japanese:** Large language models trained on Japanese data are changing how Japanese is written and processed. Autocorrect and input method editors (IME — 入力メソッドエディタ) have already changed how people choose kanji (type phonetically, select from suggestions).

The language has been changing for 1,500 years. It will keep changing. Your job as a learner is to learn it as it is now — and enjoy the traces of everything it has been.
