# Traditional Music — 日本の伝統音楽

> *"Japanese traditional music doesn't follow Western scales or time signatures. It follows breath, nature, and space. Once you hear it with that context, it becomes something else entirely."*

Japan's traditional music is ancient, diverse, and still alive — performed at shrines, festivals, theaters, and concert halls. Understanding it enriches cultural knowledge, unlocks JLPT reading passages, and helps you appreciate things that are deeply embedded in the Japanese aesthetic.

---

## The Aesthetics of Japanese Music

Before jumping into instruments and genres, understand the underlying aesthetic principles:

**間 (ma):** Silence is music. The pause between notes is deliberate and meaningful. Japanese music breathes.

**わびさび (wabi-sabi):** Imperfection has beauty. A single quivering flute note, asymmetrical rhythm, a voice that cracks slightly — these aren't failures, they're expression.

**自然 (shizen — nature):** Japanese music often imitates or evokes natural sounds: rain, wind, flowing water, birds, insects. The shakuhachi (bamboo flute) is specifically designed to produce breath-like, natural tones.

## 雅楽 — Gagaku (Court Music)

**雅楽 (gagaku)** is the oldest surviving orchestral music in the world, performed continuously for over 1,300 years. It's the court music of the Japanese imperial household.

### What It Sounds Like
Slow, spacious, alien to Western ears. The tempo is glacially slow by Western standards — a single phrase can last a minute. But it creates a profound sense of suspended time.

### Instruments
| Instrument | Type | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 笙 (shō) | Wind | Mouth organ — cluster of pipes arranged in circle. Creates sustained chords. |
| 篳篥 (hichiriki) | Wind | Small double-reed pipe — the melodic lead |
| 龍笛 (ryūteki) | Wind | Transverse flute |
| 琵琶 (biwa) | String | Short-necked lute |
| 箏 (koto) | String | 13-string zither |
| 太鼓 (taiko) | Percussion | Large drum |
| 鞨鼓 (kakko) | Percussion | Small barrel drum |
| 鐃鈸 (nōhachi) | Percussion | Cymbals |

### Where to Hear It
- **宮内庁式部職楽部 (Imperial Household Agency Music Department):** Performs at imperial ceremonies
- 春日大社 (Kasuga Grand Shrine), 四天王寺 (Shitennō-ji), 住吉大社 (Sumiyoshi Taisha) — major shrine concerts

## 能楽 — Noh Music (能)

Noh theater (see [Traditional Arts](../culture/07-traditional-arts.md)) has its own musical tradition:

### Instruments (囃子 — hayashi)
| Instrument | Reading | Type |
|---|---|---|
| 能管 (nōkan) | nōkan | High-pitched flute — eerie, penetrating tone |
| 小鼓 (kotsuzumi) | kotsuzumi | Small hand drum (held at shoulder) |
| 大鼓 (ōtsuzumi) | ōtsuzumi | Larger hand drum (held at hip) |
| 太鼓 (taiko) | taiko | Large barrel drum |

### Chanting (謡 — utai)
The vocal style in Noh is **謡 (utai)** — a highly stylized chanting between singing and speech. Text is drawn from classical Japanese poetry and literature.

## 箏 — Koto

The **箏 (koto)** is Japan's iconic string instrument — a 13-string (traditionally) zither about 180cm long, played with plectrum picks (爪 — tsume) on the right hand.

**Yatsuhashi Kengyō (八橋検校, 1614–1685)** established the classical koto tradition. His piece **六段の調 (Rokudan no Shirabe)** — "Six Sections" — remains a defining work of Japanese music and appears on JLPT cultural reading passages.

### Contemporary koto
Modern composers have extended the koto dramatically:
- **宮城道雄 (Miyagi Michio, 1894–1956):** Blind koto master who modernized the instrument and composed **春の海 (Haru no Umi — "Spring Sea")** — the piece Japanese people universally associate with New Year's Day (it plays everywhere in January)
- **沢井忠夫 / 沢井一恵:** Contemporary koto composers pushing the instrument into new territory
- The koto has been played in jazz, pop, and avant-garde contexts

## 三味線 — Shamisen

The **三味線 (shamisen)** is a three-stringed banjo-like instrument, played with a large plectrum (撥 — bachi). It arrived from China via the Ryūkyū Kingdom in the 16th century and became central to Edo popular culture.

### Types and Uses
| Type | Size | Music style |
|---|---|---|
| 細棹 (hosozao) | Small neck | Nagauta (long song), used in kabuki |
| 中棹 (nakazao) | Medium neck | Minyo (folk), kouta (short song) |
| 太棹 (futozao) | Thick neck | Gidayū-bushi (joruri narrative), Tsugaru-jamisen |

### Tsugaru-Jamisen (津軽三味線)
The folk shamisen tradition from Tohoku's Tsugaru region is the most exciting for many modern listeners — rapid, aggressive, improvisational. It sounds more like electric guitar than anything you'd expect from a traditional instrument.

**木村大** and **上妻宏光 (Agatsuma Hiromitsu)** are contemporary artists who play modern fusions of Tsugaru shamisen.

## 尺八 — Shakuhachi

The **尺八 (shakuhachi)** is an end-blown bamboo flute. The name comes from its standard length (一尺八寸 — one shaku, eight sun = ~54.5cm).

Sound is produced by blowing across the top — similar to blowing across a bottle, requiring significant technique. The resulting sound is breathy, haunting, and deeply human.

Originally used by Buddhist monks of the 普化宗 (Fuke sect) as a meditation practice — playing was called **吹禅 (suizen, "blowing Zen")**.

The repertoire for solo shakuhachi includes pieces called **本曲 (honkyoku)** — original compositions specifically for the instrument, often meditative and deeply slow.

**Contemporary shakuhachi:**
The instrument has been adopted in jazz, ambient music, and global fusion. **Riley Lee** and **Kaoru Kakizakai** are internationally recognized performers who play in both classical and contemporary contexts.

## 演歌 — Enka

**演歌 (enka)** is Japan's deeply emotional popular ballad genre — often called "Japan's blues" or "Japan's country music." It emerged in the Meiji period from political song forms and became a distinct genre in the 1960s–70s.

### Characteristics
- Minor keys with pentatonic scales
- **こぶし (kobushi):** Distinctive vocal ornament — a wavering rise and fall on certain syllables, similar to vibrato but more abrupt
- **マイクの持ち方:** The distinctive way enka singers hold the microphone (slightly tilted, held close)
- Lyrics about: lost love, the cold north, sake, fishing villages, the loneliness of the road (旅 — tabi), maternal love, stoic endurance
- Visual: Traditional kimono, dramatic stage presence

### Key Artists
| Artist | Notes |
|---|---|
| 美空ひばり (Misora Hibari) | The ultimate. Her songs span kayōkyoku and enka. |
| 北島三郎 (Kitajima Saburō) | "Saburo" — Japan's most beloved male enka singer |
| 都はるみ (Miyako Harumi) | Powerful voice, emotional intensity |
| 細川たかし (Hosokawa Takashi) | 矢切の渡し — classic |
| 演歌の女王: 天童よしみ (Tendō Yoshimi) | Exceptional voice |

**Cultural note:** Enka is strongly associated with middle-aged and elderly Japanese. Being able to reference an enka song, especially Misora Hibari's, is a meaningful cultural touch when talking to older Japanese people.

### Enka for Language Learning
Enka lyrics are full of rich, sometimes classical vocabulary. Themes of longing, rain, cold nights, and sake make them linguistically rich if emotionally dramatic.

Key vocabulary from enka:
| Word | Reading | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 旅 | tabi | journey / travels |
| 演歌 | enka | enka (演 = perform, 歌 = song) |
| 望郷 | bōkyō | longing for home |
| 哀愁 | aishū | pathos / sadness |
| 盃 | sakazuki | sake cup |
| 涙 | namida | tears |
| 故郷 | furusato | hometown / birthplace |

## 民謡 — Minyo (Folk Music)

**民謡 (minyo)** is Japan's regional folk music — work songs, festival songs, sea shanties, and harvest songs. Every prefecture has distinct minyo traditions:

| Region | Famous minyo | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 沖縄 | 安里屋ユンタ (Asadoya Yunta) | Okinawan folk — distinct scale, sanshin |
| 秋田 | 秋田音頭 (Akita Ondo) | Harvest festival song |
| 北海道 | ソーラン節 (Sōran Bushi) | Herring fishermen's work song — adopted as school sports anthem |
| 鹿児島 | おはら節 (Ohara Bushi) | Kagoshima folk |
| 新潟 | 佐渡おけさ (Sado Okesa) | Island folk |

**ソーラン節 (Sōran Bushi)** has been adopted as an energetic dance performance by school students across Japan — you'll see it performed at sports festivals and school events. The rough fisherman's original has become a national youth anthem.

## 太鼓 — Taiko Drumming

**太鼓 (taiko)** is Japanese drumming, and it ranges from gentle shrine accompaniment to physically intense full-body performance.

**和太鼓 (wadaiko)** — traditional Japanese drumming:
- Used at festivals, Noh, gagaku, and religious ceremonies
- The **鼓童 (Kodō)** ensemble from Sado Island is the world's most famous taiko group — their performances are extraordinary

**Taiko vocabulary:**
| Japanese | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 長胴太鼓 (nagado taiko) | Long-bodied barrel drum (the classic big drum) |
| 桶胴太鼓 (okedo taiko) | Lightweight bucket drum, portable |
| 締太鼓 (shime taiko) | Rope-tensioned small drum |
| 鉦 (kane) | Metal gong used with taiko |
| バチ (bachi) | Drumstick |

## Traditional Music and the JLPT

Traditional music appears in JLPT N3–N1 reading and listening sections as cultural knowledge:
- Descriptions of instruments and their use
- Festival music contexts
- Historical descriptions of court or Noh music
- Regional folk culture passages

Key terms to know: 雅楽, 能楽, 歌舞伎, 民謡, 三味線, 箏, 尺八, 演歌, 太鼓

Understanding these terms — even passively — helps with cultural reading passages that trip up learners who've never encountered traditional arts vocabulary.
